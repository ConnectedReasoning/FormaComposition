# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 18
## The Complete Dominant Ninth

> *The major dominant ninth has further limitations with respect to the common practice of composers. It represents a harmonic color and style of the end of the common-practice period rather than of the eighteenth century.*

---

## The Complete V9 — Root Present

Adding the root (G) back to the incomplete dominant ninths gives the **complete dominant ninth chord**:

| Form | Notes (C major) | Symbol |
|---|---|---|
| **V9 major** | G B D F **A** | V9 |
| **V9 minor** | G B D F **Ab** | V9b |

Five notes require reducing to four for standard four-part writing. The **fifth (D) is omitted** — it is the least important factor contrapuntally (no strong tendency, doesn't define the chord's character). The characteristic sound of the dominant ninth is unaffected by the fifth's absence.

Standard four-part voicings (fifth omitted):
- **V9 major**: G B F A (root, leading-tone, seventh, ninth)
- **V9 minor**: G B F Ab (root, leading-tone, seventh, minor ninth)

---

## Spacing Constraints — Critical for the Major Ninth

The most important practical rule for the complete V9:

> *The ninth must be placed at least a ninth above the root.*

**The ninth must never be placed below the root.** This is not a voice-leading preference but a harmonic identity rule — if the ninth falls below the root, the chord loses its quality as a ninth chord and sounds like something else entirely.

**Preferred arrangement:** Leading-tone (B) below the ninth (A). This preserves the characteristic sound.

Five disposition options (from most to least desirable):
- **(a)** Root far below, ninth far above: not heard as a ninth chord (too spread)
- **(b)** Root above ninth: **avoid** — loses ninth chord identity
- **(c)** Ninth below leading-tone: characteristic ninth sound present, not ideal
- **(d)** Fifth omitted, leading-tone below ninth: **the most typical and useful arrangement**
- **(e)** Leading-tone present and below ninth: **the best arrangement** — ninth chord character fully preserved

The peculiarly strident effect of extreme close voicings (all factors within an octave) is due to spacing — the resulting interval of a second is not characteristic of this chord.

---

## The Three Treatments of the Ninth

Piston identifies three ways composers handle the ninth in practice, from most ornamented to most harmonic:

### 1. As a Nonharmonic Melodic Tone
The ninth appears as any type of nonharmonic tone — appoggiatura, passing tone, auxiliary — and resolves into the fifth degree (D→G in C) before the chord itself resolves to the tonic. The ninth "visits" the chord but doesn't fully integrate harmonically. This is the most common treatment, especially in the early part of the common-practice period.

> *"It is often an appoggiatura, in which case the harmonic color is very pronounced, but even when it is rhythmically weak its harmonic significance is recognized by the ear."*

### 2. As an Unresolved Harmonic Factor
The ninth is used as a true chord tone but **absent from the chord at the moment of change** to the tonic. The ninth sounds as part of V9, then the chord changes to I — but the ninth doesn't visibly resolve; it simply disappears. This implies a slowly moving harmonic rhythm compared to the melodic activity above. The ninth functions as an "overtone" of the dominant sonority, too high in the series to need explicit resolution. This treatment became common only in the **latter part of the nineteenth century**.

### 3. As a Normal Dissonant Chord Tone Resolving
The ninth acts as a fully harmonic dissonance and resolves in the normal way — descending by step to the octave of the tonic. This is the most theoretically regular treatment but comparatively rare in classical writing. Parallel motion of ninth chords (seen in late 19th century Liszt, Schubert) belongs to this category — these should be understood as exceptional cases of a procedure that became more exploited in the twentieth century.

---

## Major vs. Minor Ninth

- **Minor ninth (V9b)**: resolves to either a major or a minor tonic — equally appropriate to both modes
- **Major ninth (V9 major)**: used **only before the major tonic**. The A natural would create a cross-relation in a minor context.

This means the complete major ninth is **primarily a major-mode chord**, while the complete minor ninth is more universally applicable.

---

## Inversions

The complete ninth chord in inversion is rare in the common-practice period. Occasional examples exist. Rules for inversion:
- The spacing restrictions regarding relative positions of ninth, root, and leading-tone still apply
- The **fourth inversion** (ninth in the bass) is not used — this would place the ninth below the root, violating the fundamental spacing rule
- The **second inversion** (fifth in bass) is less used than the first and third inversions

In figured bass, the Arabic numerals simply identify which notes are used; the required spacing is not reflected in the figured bass itself.

---

## Secondary Dominants

The complete dominant ninth chord is used as a secondary dominant with the limitation that **the major ninth is not used to introduce a minor tonic**. The most common secondary applications:
- **V9ofIV** — very common (C minor ninth version = dominant of F major; C major ninth = approaches F major)
- **V9ofV** — approaches the dominant
- **V9ofVI** — approaches the submediant

Irregular resolutions of secondary dominant ninths are occasionally employed. The complete ninth chord's root prevents the enharmonic flexibility that made the incomplete minor ninth (dim7) so versatile as a modulation pivot.

---

## Modulation

The ninth chord has the same disadvantage as other dominant chords: **the pivot chord should preferably not be the dominant of the new key**. Therefore it is more effectively used as a secondary dominant in the second key — appearing just before the new key is firmly established, acting as V9 of some chord in the new key rather than as the main dominant.

The presence of the root rules out the enharmonic changes possible with the incomplete minor ninth. For modulation, the dim7 remains more flexible.

---

## FormaComposition Implications

**The spacing rule is absolute: ninth always above root by at least a ninth.** For FormaComposition's voicing logic, when generating a V9 chord, the ninth (A in C major, Ab in C minor) must sit at least nine semitones above the root (G). Any voicing that places A below G or even within the same octave as G requires either a two-octave span or simply omitting the fifth and carefully arranging the remaining factors.

**The most practical use is as an appoggiatura (treatment 1).** Generating a V9 in FormaComposition is most idiomatically done by having the ninth appear as a melodic decoration — the melody or inner voice steps up to A at the moment of V, then resolves to G before the chord changes to I. This requires coordination between the melody/counterpoint voice and the harmony voice.

**The minor ninth (V9b) is more flexible than the major (V9).** For any section in a minor key that needs the tension of a complete dominant ninth, use V9b. The major form (V9) stays in major contexts.

**V9 signals late Romantic harmonic style.** The complete dominant ninth chord with the ninth treated harmonically (not as a nonharmonic tone) is characteristic of music from 1850 onward — Liszt, Wagner, late Brahms. Using it in a piece targeted at a Brian Eno–style ambient aesthetic would be an anachronism — though not wrong, it carries stylistic associations. The incomplete forms (dim7 and half-dim) are more period-neutral.

**Treatment 2 (ninth absent at resolution) is the ambient-appropriate version.** A V9 chord held for several bars with melodic activity in upper voices, then dissolving to I without the ninth visibly resolving — this is the slowly moving harmonic rhythm treatment Piston describes. This texture maps directly to long sustained pads with melodic ornamentation above, which is exactly the ambient texture FormaComposition generates.
