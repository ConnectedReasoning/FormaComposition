# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 21
## Ninth, Eleventh, and Thirteenth Chords

> *The omission of factors weakens the sense of structure in thirds and allows the ear to accept the higher factors as melodic tones dependent on a simple harmonic background, usually a triad, or dominant seventh chord.*

---

## The Central Reality

It would seem natural to extend harmonic vocabulary by continuing to superpose thirds: if seventh chords, why not ninths, elevenths, thirteenths? But:

> *"Conscious efforts to accomplish this, however, are not observed in the works of composers of the common-practice period in harmony."*

These chords arise — but almost never as primary harmonic events. They arise as **contrapuntal coincidences**: appoggiaturae, suspensions, passing tones, and pedal tones creating momentary vertical combinations that theorists label as "ninth chords" or "eleventh chords." The label is analytically convenient but can mislead: calling a passing tone a "chord factor" overstates its harmonic independence.

Piston's key principle: the **omission of factors** is what allows these chord labels to work at all. A true thirteenth chord (all seven scale degrees stacked in thirds) would be so thick and chromatic that no ear could parse it harmonically. In practice, several inner factors are always missing — and the missing factors are precisely the ones that would force the ear to hear the sonority as a pure chord rather than as contrapuntal decoration over a simpler harmony.

---

## The Nondominant Ninth

Nondominant ninth chord effects are found mainly on roots **I, II, and IV**. They arise in nearly all cases from appoggiaturae or suspensions.

**The Appoggiatura:** A note one step above the octave of the bass (= a ninth interval) creates the effect of a ninth chord.
- Without the seventh: the fundamental harmony is a **triad in root position** — the ninth is simply a melodic appoggiatura
- With the seventh: when seventh and ninth both appear as double appoggiaturas, the fundamental harmony is a **chord of the sixth** (sixth above bass = tonal chord a third above)

**Appoggiatura with Delayed Resolution:** When the appoggiatura or suspension delays resolution past the change of harmony, the nondominant ninth seems to possess more harmonic independence. This greater independence is an illusion — the contrapuntal origin is still apparent — but it gives the chord a more characteristically harmonic feeling.

**The Unresolved Appoggiatura:** At the final stage, the contrapuntal tone is left completely unresolved. This is essential in eleventh and thirteenth chord effects. The character of the higher factors as contrapuntal tones **whose resolution is not sounded but implied** must be recognized. This is different from the dominant ninth's treatment: in nondominant contexts, the unresolved tone is understood but not explicitly resolved.

**Nondominant ninth roots**: I9, II9, IV9 are common; III9 and VI9 are little used.

## The Pedal (Organ Point)

The pedal is a **sustained tone in one voice** while the harmony above changes. It tends to render the harmonic rhythm static — usually offset by using dissonant harmonies above it that are foreign to the pedal tone.

> *"A good pedal will be at some moment foreign to the harmony with which it sounds, and it customarily begins and ends as a member of the harmony."*

Pedal tones may appear:
- In the **bass** (most common) — the "organ pedal" or "pedal point"
- In an **inner voice** — creates a different texture
- In the **upper voice** — a sustained high note above changing harmony

By far the most common pedals are on the **tonic** and **dominant**:

### Tonic Pedal
The dominant seventh chord above a tonic pedal creates the **tonic eleventh chord** effect — the fundamental harmony is V7, but with the tonic note persisting in the bass. The upper harmony sounds dissonant against the pedal; the effect is one of accumulating tension above a stable foundation.

> *"The tonic eleventh effect may be created by a dominant seventh chord superimposed upon a tonic pedal."*

In rhythm, this is often "five over one" — the dominant harmony's accent pattern against the tonic pedal's sustain.

### Dominant Pedal  
The "dominant eleventh" commonly referred to is actually the **subdominant triad sounding over a dominant pedal**. The subdominant (IV) is naturally dissonant with the dominant (V) bass — a second apart. The suspended texture above a dominant pedal is one of the most characteristic sounds of the common-practice period, especially in slow movements and approaches to cadences.

The combination may have the dominant pedal above **and** below the subdominant chord simultaneously — creating a kind of dominant bracket around the subdominant harmony.

### Double Pedal
Both tonic and dominant pedals held simultaneously, with harmonies moving freely above. This reinforces the key so strongly that the accompanying harmony can range widely without destroying tonal unity. In modern developments, the double pedal is considered one of the origins of polytonality — two keys represented simultaneously by pedal and harmony.

## The Chord of the Eleventh

True eleventh chord effects (beyond the pedal mechanism) arise from **appoggiaturas and suspensions**. The eleventh is most typically found as a nonharmonic tone over a dominant or subdominant basis.

The **dominant eleventh** (the "IV over V" pedal) is the overwhelmingly common form.

**II and IV** can also show eleventh effects via appoggiatura with delayed resolution.

**The eleventh may appear as a nonharmonic tone over the dominant ninth** — an additional layer of dissonance above an already complex dominant harmony.

---

## The Chord of the Thirteenth

> *"The thirteenth is as high as it is possible to go in the series of thirds, since the fifteenth would coincide with the double octave."*

The dominant thirteenth chord effect: the **third degree** sounds above the dominant. This is actually the third degree that "tends to be absorbed into the dominant harmony when it stands above a dominant bass, as in III6 and I64." It is the same note that gives rise to the term "dominant thirteenth."

**As appoggiatura:** The thirteenth appears as an appoggiatura (a sixth above the bass), resolving to the fifth of the chord. When unresolved, the appoggiatura stays as a contrapuntal tone while the harmony resolves — the resolution is implied, not sounded.

**As a complete tonic thirteenth:** Formed by a combination of the dominant ninth above a tonic pedal — the dominant is represented by four suspensions which subsequently resolve into the tonic harmony. This creates the fullest possible presentation of the thirteenth.

**The dominant ninth used with a tonic pedal** creates a "thirteenth chord" effect — the ninth of V above the tonic bass = the tenth = double third = which inverted is a thirteenth. This may use either major or minor ninth, with or without the root of V.

---

## Practical Summary: What These Chords Actually Are

| Label | What it usually is |
|---|---|
| Nondominant ninth | Appoggiatura or suspension above a triad or V6 |
| Tonic eleventh | V7 above a tonic pedal |
| Dominant eleventh | IV (subdominant) above a dominant pedal |
| Dominant thirteenth | Third degree as appoggiatura to fifth, above V |
| Complete tonic thirteenth | V9 above a tonic pedal, suspensions resolving |

The "chord labels" (I9, V11, V13) are analytical shorthand for contrapuntal combinations. The underlying harmonic reality is almost always simpler.

## FormaComposition Implications

**The pedal point is the FormaComposition mechanism for creating these chord effects.** Rather than generating V11 or V13 as specific chord types, the engine should:
1. Assign a bass pedal note (tonic or dominant)
2. Allow upper voices to move through dissonant harmonies above it
3. Label the result as a "pedal texture"

This is more compositionally accurate than treating I11 or V13 as independent chord entities.

**The tonic pedal with V7 above = one of the most useful ambient textures.** In a slow section with long harmonic rhythm, holding the tonic bass while the upper voices cycle through V7 (and its inversions) creates the classic "V over I" sustained texture. This is the sound of the opening of many slow movements — a kind of harmonic stasis with upper-voice dissonance that resolves very slowly.

**The dominant pedal with IV above = the pre-cadential suspension texture.** The subdominant chord above a dominant pedal is one of the most characteristic sounds approaching a cadence. In FormaComposition, this can be implemented as a bass holding on G while the upper voices play F A C — a dissonant preparation for the final V→I.

**Nondominant ninth effects arise naturally from the existing voice leading.** If the melody or counterpoint voice holds a note while the harmony changes beneath it, a ninth effect is created automatically. The engine doesn't need to explicitly generate ninth chords — it needs good voice leading that occasionally creates melodic holdovers.

**"Unresolved appoggiatura" is the ambient music default.** The unresolved contrapuntal tone — left hanging rather than explicitly resolving — is exactly the texture that Valhalla Shimmer and sustained pads create acoustically. The reverb tail holds the previous note while the harmony moves on. Piston gives this a theoretical name: it is not an error or an accident but a recognized technique with a 200-year lineage.
