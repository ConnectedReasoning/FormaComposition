ex1 — authentic cadence in three grades of strength: plain V–I, then I64–V–I, then the full II6–I64–V–I. The progression from minimal to maximal closure is clearly audible — each version sounds more settled than the last.
ex2 — perfect vs imperfect authentic cadences back to back: V root pos → I with tonic in soprano (maximum finality); V6 → I (leading tone in bass, less final); V → I with third in soprano (gentle close). The same V→I gesture, three different emotional weights.
ex3 — four half cadence approaches: IV–V, II6–V, IV–I64–V (with 6/4 accentuation), VofV–V (dominant of dominant). The VofV version is the brightest and most expectant — that chromatic F# in the penultimate chord sharpens the cadential point.
ex4 — plagal cadences: authentic then plagal "Amen," then the same with minor IV (that Ab chord arriving after the major tonic is one of the most characteristic sounds of 19th century closes), then a standalone plagal phrase. The minor plagal version is worth sitting with — it's the sound of a hundred Romantic endings.
ex5 — deceptive cadences: V–VI (the standard interrupted cadence with its surprise), V–IV6, then a full musical context where the deceptive cadence delays the final close and allows an extra phrase to arrive before the authentic resolution. This is the structural function of the deceptive cadence at its most useful.
ex6 — the complete two-phrase period: antecedent (half cadence, suspended) then consequent (full authentic). This is the fundamental sentence structure of tonal music — question and answer. It's the template for most 8-bar sections.
ex7 — cadential extension: a phrase reaching its first V–I, then immediately repeating V–I, then closing with the full II6–I64–V–I. The repetition signals "this is the ending" more emphatically than a single cadence. Symphonic movements do this for tens of bars.
ex8 — all four types in sequence: half, perfect authentic, deceptive, then plagal-after-authentic. This is the complete cadential vocabulary of tonal harmony in one listening session.
