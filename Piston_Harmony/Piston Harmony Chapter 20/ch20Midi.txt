ex1 — all six nondominant seventh chords in sequence, each preceded by its simple triad for reference: I7 (major seventh — bright, slightly sharp), II7 (minor seventh — the familiar pre-dominant), III7, IV7 (major seventh — warm), VI7, VII7 (half-diminished). Hearing each against its own triad makes the seventh's character audible.
ex2 — each seventh resolving to its regular target: I7→IV, II7→V→I, III7→VI, IV7→II7→V→I (the indirect route), VI7→II, VII7→III. The indirect resolution of IV7 is particularly important — it never goes directly to VII, always steps through the intermediate II7.
ex3 — the money comparison: plain II–V–I, then II7–V–I, then the full II7–I64–V7–I. Loading these back to back in Logic with any lush sound makes the argument for always using II7 over plain II at cadences — it's richer without adding complexity.
ex4 — I7 versus VofIV: the same C-in-bass chord with major seventh (C E G B) versus minor seventh (C E G Bb). Both resolve to IV. The major seventh version (I7, nondominant) is smooth and coloristic; the minor seventh version (VofIV, secondary dominant) has more directed tension. One semitone, two harmonic identities.
ex5 — the descending-fifths sequence of all seven seventh chords: I7–IV7–VII7–III7–VI7–II7–V7–I. This is one of the most kinetically satisfying passages in tonal music — eight bars of continuous mild dissonance, every root moving down a fifth, every seventh resolving by step. It's the Baroque "harmonic engine" running at full capacity.
ex6 — VI7 in context: plain I–VI–IV–II6–V–I versus I–VI7–II7–V–I. The VI7 has a particular warm, open quality (A C E G in C major) that the plain VI triad doesn't carry.
ex7 — modulation via nondominant pivot: II7 of C (D F A C) reinterpreted in A minor context. The nondominant seventh's tonal ambiguity makes the key change smooth — no dominant "announcement."
ex8 — the complete phrase using I7, VI7, II7 in sequence: I → I7 → IV → VI7 → II7 → I64 → V7 → I, then I7 → IV → I for the plagal close. Three different nondominant sevenths in one phrase, each adding a layer of color without disrupting the tonal logic.
