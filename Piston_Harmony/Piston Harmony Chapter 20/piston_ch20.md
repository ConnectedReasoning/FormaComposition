# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 20
## Nondominant Harmony — Seventh Chords

> *Harmony of dominant effect and triads make up the bulk of the harmonic material in the common-practice period. Dissonant chords of nondominant character are found to be comparatively unusual, when the whole period is considered.*

---

## What Nondominant Harmony Is

Dominant harmony has specific external features:
- The **leading-tone** a major third above the root
- A **diminished fifth** above the leading-tone

These intervals create the characteristic "dominant" sound and the tendency toward resolution. Chords that lack these features — even when they are seventh chords — have **nondominant character**.

Using only the tones of the major and minor scales, seventh chords can be constructed on every scale degree other than the dominant:

| Chord | Major scale form | Minor scale forms |
|---|---|---|
| I7 | Major triad + major 7th | — |
| II7 | Minor triad + minor 7th | Multiple forms |
| III7 | Minor triad + minor 7th | Multiple forms |
| IV7 | Major triad + minor 7th | Multiple forms |
| VI7 | Minor triad + minor 7th | Multiple forms |
| VII7 | Diminished triad + minor 7th | — (usually dominant) |

The variants (a, b, c, d in Piston's table) arise from the different forms of the minor scale: harmonic, melodic ascending, melodic descending, and combined forms. This gives some degrees multiple seventh chord forms, all diatonic.

**II7b, IV7c, VI7c, and VII7** are similar in interval construction to dominant forms. The true identity of these chords becomes clear only from their resolution.

**Five chords form a group** with minor third, perfect fifth, and minor seventh (minor seventh chord quality): I7c, II7a, III7a, IV7b, VI7a.

**Four chords** have major third, perfect fifth, major seventh (major seventh chord quality — sharp, pungent dissonance): I7a, III7c, IV7a, VI7b.

**Two groupings of two chords each** with minor third, perfect fifth, major seventh (minor-major): I7b, IV7d. And with major third, augmented fifth, major seventh (augmented): III7b, VI7d.

---

## The Nonharmonic Element

A key principle of this chapter:

> *"All chords are the product of the momentary coincidence of melodic parts. A vertical cross-section of the music at a given instant is undeniably a chord, but we notice that some of these chords are constantly recurring under different conditions whereas others seem to depend on some other simpler chord for their existence."*

Some nondominant seventh chords are so closely tied to simpler chords (through voice leading) that calling them independent seventh chords overstates their harmonic status. The question is always: is the seventh an independent harmonic factor, or a nonharmonic tone (appoggiatura, suspension, passing tone) above a simpler chord?

The analysis can go to absurd extremes in either direction — treating every vertical coincidence as a complex chord, or reducing everything to appoggiaturae on simple triads. Practice lies between.

---

## Resolution

The resolution rule is **identical to dominant seventh chord resolution**:

> *"The seventh of the chord resolves customarily by moving down one degree."*

The harmonic regular resolution: **to the chord whose root is a perfect fourth higher** (same strong root motion as V→I). Exceptions:
- **IV7**: regular resolution would be to VII — not used; instead seventh descends through the other chord tones, making a form of II7, which proceeds to V
- **Minor VI7**: the perfect fourth above would lie outside the scale — so it resolves differently

**Irregular resolutions** are also possible but less common than the dominant seventh's wide variety of irregular options.

When resolving to another seventh chord, one of the seventh chords is usually incomplete (root doubled, fifth omitted).

**Inversions** resolve contrapuntally in the same way as root position, except the root usually remains stationary rather than moving up a fourth.

---

## The Six Nondominant Seventh Chords

### I7 — The Tonic Seventh
**Regular resolution: to IV.** The seventh (the leading-tone, or major seventh) of I7 descends by step to the major sixth degree, which is a factor of IV.

- The seventh occurs often as an **appoggiatura** — it steps down to the root of IV without being treated as a fully harmonic factor
- Also found frequently at the cadence: **I7→IV** as part of a plagal cadential extension
- In minor, the lowered seventh degree is used in order to descend melodically to the minor sixth degree

The tonic seventh is already well-known from Ch. 14 as **VofIV** — that is its secondary dominant interpretation. As a nondominant chord, it has a different quality: less directed, more coloristic.

### II7 — The Supertonic Seventh
**Regular resolution: to V.** Very common in cadences, before the dominant or the cadential six-four chord. 

- The minor form (D F A C in C major) is used in both major and minor modes
- The major form (with major seventh) is used only in major surroundings
- Irregular resolutions are to I, III, VI, and secondary dominants
- The seventh often acts as **appoggiatura to the octave of the root** of the next chord
- The commonest irregular resolution is to **II7 itself** — root remains static, becoming the seventh of the next chord (a beautiful voice-leading continuity)

### III7 — The Mediant Seventh
**Regular resolution: to VI.** The fifth of III7, when it is the leading-tone, ascends; when it is the lowered seventh degree, it descends.

- Irregular resolutions are to IV, II, and secondary dominants
- In its minor form with the augmented fifth, creates a characteristic exotic color

### IV7 — The Subdominant Seventh
The regular resolution of IV7 **is not to the root a fourth above** (which would be VII — not used). Instead:
- The seventh **moves down through the other chord tones**, making a form of II7 which then proceeds to V
- The seventh is therefore usually an **appoggiatura**

The direct resolution to V is less common and requires care to avoid parallel fifths. When IV7 does go directly to V, the six-four chord is often interposed to smooth the voice leading.

Irregular resolutions are to I and several secondary dominants, including an unusual parallel-motion resolution to III7.

### VI7 — The Submediant Seventh
**Regular resolution: to II.** When the third of VI7 is the leading-tone, it is prolonged or repeated, becoming the seventh of II7. The two chords may sound like both IV and II simultaneously.

- The form with major sixth degree and minor third degree (combining qualities from ascending and descending melodic minor) is the most characteristic of the submediant seventh in minor
- Irregular resolution to a subdominant chord with chromatically raised root is also found

### VII7 — The Leading-Tone Seventh
This chord usually functions as the incomplete dominant minor ninth (Ch. 16–17) and partakes of dominant characteristics. However, when it proceeds to **III**, it has a nondominant quality:
- The progression VII7→III is found especially in sequences
- In this context, the chord loses its identity as a dominant ninth and becomes simply the seventh chord on the leading-tone resolving to the mediant

## Nondominant Sevenths in Sequence

The continuous series of seventh chords is a **favorite device in nonmodulating sequences**. The sevenths enter as suspensions; when chords are in root position, every other chord is incomplete (root doubled). All inversions may be employed.

The descending-fifths sequence of seventh chords (I7–IV7–VII7–III7–VI7–II7–V7–I) is one of the most characteristic and kinetic passages in Baroque and Classical harmonic writing. Bach uses it constantly.

---

## Modulation

Nondominant seventh chords are **useful as pivot chords** in modulation precisely because they don't strongly suggest any particular key. Their nondominant character means the modulation doesn't carry the "announcement" of a dominant chord.

The II7 of C major can be interpreted as:
- I7 of D minor
- III7 of Bb major
- IV7 of A minor
- VI7 of F major

These multiple interpretations — without needing enharmonic change — make nondominant sevenths quietly versatile modulation pivots. Irregular resolutions can confirm the interpretation in the second key.

## FormaComposition Implications

**Nondominant seventh chords are the "color" layer that most generated progressions lack.** Diatonic progressions without seventh chords sound harmonically thin; dominant sevenths (V7) provide direction but not variety; nondominant sevenths provide harmonic richness without the directional urgency of dominant harmony. They are the chords that make progressions feel composed rather than functional.

**II7 is the most important nondominant seventh in cadential context.** II7–V–I (or II7–I64–V–I) is the full cadential formula with richer color than II–V–I. FormaComposition should default to II7 rather than II at cadences when targeting a richer harmonic texture.

**I7 pointing to IV is the VofIV from Ch. 14 — the same chord, two analytical identities.** As VofIV it is a secondary dominant with chromatic tension; as I7 it is a nondominant seventh with coloristic function. The choice of analysis affects how the melody and continuation are treated. FormaComposition should know this double identity.

**The descending-fifths seventh-chord sequence** (I7–IV7–VII7–III7–VI7–II7–V7–I) is among the most useful generation templates. It produces eight chords of continuous forward motion, all within the key, all with the mild dissonance of seventh chords. A single FormaComposition section could be built entirely on this sequence.

**Nondominant sevenths as pivot chords produce smooth, unannounced modulations.** Using II7 (= IV7 of the relative minor, or VI7 of another key) as a pivot creates a key change that the ear accepts as a natural continuation rather than an abrupt shift. This is the tool for creating the "seamless" modulations characteristic of late Romantic style.
