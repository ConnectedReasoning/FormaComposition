ex1 — the cadential 6/4 in three versions: plain V–I, then I–I64–V–I, then the full II6–I64–V–I. The key thing to hear is that the bass doesn't move across the 6/4 to V — it holds on G the entire time. Only the upper voices change. This reveals that you're really listening to V with decorations the whole time.
ex2 — appoggiatura vs suspension version of the same cadential 6/4. First version: 6/4 arrives on a strong beat, V comes after on a weaker beat (strong-to-weak). Second version: 6/4 slips in on the weak beat, V arrives on the strong beat (weak-to-strong). Same pitches, rhythmically opposite.
ex3 — noncadential IV64 mid-phrase: hear it appear on a strong beat inside the phrase rather than at the cadence. Then the same phrase without it — the 6/4 adds a particular lean and weight that the bare IV doesn't have.
ex4 — auxiliary 6/4: bass holds on C the entire time, upper voices step up from E→F and G→A, then step back. The fourth and sixth arrive from below — that's the diagnostic feature.
ex5 — passing 6/4: bass is the passing tone (D between C and E in the bass line), upper voices are held. The 6/4 chord is rhythmically inconspicuous — it's just the bass walking through.
ex6 — all four types back-to-back for direct comparison. The rhythmic weight difference between the first two (strong) and last two (weak) is the whole point of the chapter.
ex7 — the complete classical cadential formula: I–VI–IV–II6–I64–V–I. This is the thing. ex7 is the sound of a thousand concerto cadenzas, hymn endings, and sonata movements. If FormaComposition generates this correctly, authentic cadences will feel right.
ex8 — the Schumann suspension effect: an A minor phrase resolves normally to root position tonic, then the same phrase leaves on the I64 instead. That hanging, unresolved quality is a deliberate compositional choice — the piece ends on an unstable chord, which Schumann considered more expressive than finality.
