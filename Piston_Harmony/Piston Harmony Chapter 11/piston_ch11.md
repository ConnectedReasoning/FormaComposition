# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 11
## The Six-Four Chord

> *The six-four chord is an unstable chord. It is the product of vertical coincidence of moving voices. When correctly used it can be analyzed as a grouping of nonharmonic tones.*

---

## What the Six-Four Chord Is

When a triad's **fifth** is in the bass (second inversion), the intervals between the bass and upper voices are a sixth and a fourth. The symbol is **6/4**.

The 6/4 chord is inherently **unstable** because:
- The fourth is a dissonant interval (Ch. 1)
- The sixth is only an imperfect consonance

Unlike root position or first inversion triads, the 6/4 chord should not be treated as a stable harmonic event. It is best understood as a **grouping of nonharmonic tones** — the "extra" tones (sixth and fourth above the bass) are melodic decorations of the underlying real harmony, which the bass reveals.

In four-part writing: **double the bass** — it is the inactive tone and also the real harmonic root.

---

## The Four Types

### 1. The Cadential Six-Four Chord
**By far the most common use.** The tonic triad in second inversion before the dominant at a cadence.

**What it really is:** V chord with two appoggiaturas:
- The **fourth** above the bass (scale degree 1, the root of I) is an appoggiatura resolving **down** to the **third** of V
- The **sixth** above the bass (scale degree 3, the third of I) is an appoggiatura resolving **down** to the **fifth** of V

Bass = G (= root of V), held constant. The "tonic" notes above it lean on the dominant before giving way.

```
I(6/4) → V
 C        B    (4th → 3rd of V, step down)
 E        D    (6th → 5th of V, step down)
 G        G    (bass held: real root of V throughout)
 G        G    (bass doubled)
```

**Rhythmic character:** STRONG-TO-WEAK. The 6/4 marks a **strong beat**; the V it resolves to is comparatively weaker. The bar-line is most often placed just before the 6/4.

**Approach:** Best preceded by chords that naturally lead to the dominant — **IV, II, II6, I** (giving a weak-to-strong approach into the 6/4). Chords III or V are less appropriate because they give a strong-to-weak or static rhythmic approach.

**As suspensions:** The sixth and fourth can also be suspensions (tied over) rather than appoggiaturas. In that case the harmonic rhythm reverses: the 6/4 appears on a weak beat and the V arrives on a strong beat (weak-to-strong).

**Exception:** When the dominant contains a seventh, the sixth may sometimes **rise** to the seventh (C→D over a G bass) rather than falling. This is an exception to the normal downward resolution.

### 2. The Appoggiatura Six-Four Chord (Noncadential)
The tonic 6/4 can appear **mid-phrase** as a strong rhythmic effect, detached from its cadential function. The cadential impression is avoided when such a chord occurs early in the phrase and is bypassed by melodic continuity in the voices.

The same logic applies to **IV(6/4)** and other 6/4 formations on other degrees — any triad can appear in second inversion with the same appoggiatura effect, provided the bass note is the real harmonic root and the upper tones lean into their resolution.

### 3. The Auxiliary Six-Four Chord
**Rhythmically WEAK.** The root of a triad remains stationary while its **third and fifth rise by one step and return**:

```
I → IV(6/4) → I
C:  C    F    C     (sixth = F, auxiliary rising from E)
    E    A    E     (fourth = A... wait — over a C bass, 4th = F, 6th = A)
    C    C    C     (bass held on C throughout)
```

The sixth and fourth **must enter from below** — they arrive by upward step (auxiliary motion) and return the same way. This is what makes the type identifiable: both the fourth and sixth approach from below.

A simple analysis of one root for all three chords is always possible, with the sixth and fourth acting as nonharmonic tones.

### 4. The Passing Six-Four Chord
**Rhythmically WEAK.** Here it is the **bass** that is the passing tone, not the upper voices. The bass passes between two tones a **third apart** that belong to the same underlying harmony:

```
I → V(6/4) → I   (over a stepwise bass: C → B → C? No...)
```

More typically: bass moves by step while upper voices hold. The upper voices enter and leave by step, so:
- The **sixth** above the bass = auxiliary tone
- The **fourth** above the bass = harmony note  
- The **bass itself** = passing tone

"Six-four chords of contrapuntal origin are frequent and can be understood as groupings of any of the nonharmonic tones."

The bass touching the fifth incidentally during melodic movement (as in a broken chord figure) does **not** create a true six-four chord — the decision is made on rhythmic grounds.

## Summary Table

| Type | Rhythmic Weight | Nature of 6th and 4th | Typical Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cadential** | **Strong** | Appoggiaturas resolving down | Before V at a cadence |
| **Appoggiatura** | **Strong** | Appoggiaturas, mid-phrase | Strong beat, early in phrase |
| **Auxiliary** | Weak | Auxiliary tones from below | Stationary bass, ornamental |
| **Passing** | Weak | Bass is the passing tone | Stepwise bass motion |

## The Six-Four as Suspense

Schumann ended his Romance Op.28 No.2 on a six-four chord deliberately — preferring the sense of suspension and irresolution to the finality of a root position tonic. The six-four chord's unstable quality can itself be a compositional end, not just a means.

---

## Common Formulae

The most important formulae to internalize:

- `II6–I(6/4)–V–I` — the complete cadential formula; the cleanest cadential approach
- `IV–I(6/4)–V–I` — subdominant approach to cadential 6/4
- `II–I(6/4)–V–I` — root-position supertonic approach
- `I–IV(6/4)–I` — auxiliary 6/4 on IV (F–A above C bass as auxiliary motion)
- `I–V(6/4)–I` — passing 6/4 (bass moves through 5th)

---

## FormaComposition Implications

**The cadential 6/4 is the most important formula in the cadential vocabulary.** It belongs in any section that ends with an authentic cadence. The pattern `II6–I(6/4)–V–I` is the standard classical cadential formula. FormaComposition's cadence logic should know this as a named pattern — not just V–I, but the full approach.

**The 6/4 is not a stable chord — never treat it as a resting point.** If the engine assigns a second inversion as a section's final chord, that is wrong (unless deliberately doing a Schumann-style suspension ending). Second inversions must resolve; they cannot stand alone.

**The bass doubling rule is a specific constraint.** In a 6/4 chord, the bass must be doubled. If FormaComposition's voicing logic doubles the wrong tone in a second inversion, the result will be harmonically incorrect even if it sounds close.

**The auxiliary 6/4 is a named texture effect.** The pattern where upper voices rise by step and return while the bass holds (I→IV64→I) produces a characteristic shimmering effect over a pedal — which is closely related to what reverb tails do acoustically. Naming it gives a compositional framework for what might otherwise be accidental.

**The type of 6/4 is determined by rhythm, not just notes.** The same chord (say, C-E-G with G in bass) can be cadential (strong beat, before V), auxiliary (weak beat, upper voices step up and down), or passing (weak beat, bass is the moving voice). FormaComposition needs to decide which use it's making — and schedule the chord accordingly in `harmony_pattern`.
