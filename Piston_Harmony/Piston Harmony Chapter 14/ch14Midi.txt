ex1 — VofV in three grades: plain IV–V–I, then IV–VofV–V–I (D major before G), then IV–V7ofV–V–I (D7 before G, even more directed). The F-sharp in the VofV chord sharpening toward the G arrival is immediately audible.
ex2 — VofIV: the tonic seventh chord going to the subdominant. Plain I–IV–V–I, then I–VofIV–IV–V–I (that Bb in the tonic chord pointing to F is the most available secondary dominant), then a plagal close prefaced with VofIV — the characteristic Romantic subdominant-emphasis ending.
ex3 — VofII: A major (or A7) tonicizing D. Plain II6–V–I compared to VofII–II–V–I. The C-sharp in the A major chord is the tell — it's a note that doesn't belong in C major but pulls naturally toward D.
ex4 — VofVI: E major pointing to A minor. The G-sharp is the chromatic alteration (raised tonic). The last section shows VofVI feeding a deceptive cadence — the secondary dominant pre-weights the VI arrival, making V–VI feel less like a surprise.
ex5 — VofIII: the most chromatic of the common secondaries (raises both D and F to D# and F#). The two raised notes resolve upward simultaneously into the E chord, creating a bright, slightly surprising arrival at III.
ex6 — the chain of secondary dominants: I–IV as setup, then VofIII–VofVI–VofII–VofV–V7–I. Each chord resolves into the next as a temporary tonic. This is one of the most kinetic harmonic sequences in the vocabulary — six consecutive dominant resolutions before the real I arrives. The sense of accumulating forward momentum is unmistakable.
ex7 — the money file: the same I–VI–IV–II–V–I progression twice, first plain, then with a secondary dominant preceding each diatonic chord (VofVI before VI, VofIV before IV, V7ofII before II, VofV in the cadential approach). Same skeleton, completely different harmonic texture.
ex8 — a complete two-phrase period using secondary dominants strategically: the first phrase uses VofVI and VofIV for color plus V7ofV to sharpen the cadence; the second phrase adds VofIV before the plagal close.
