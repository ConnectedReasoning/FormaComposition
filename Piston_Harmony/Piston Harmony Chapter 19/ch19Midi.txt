ex1 — the core concept: same harmonic territory, first as a plain non-sequential progression (I IV IIm V7 I), then as the same chords organized as a sequence descending by seconds (I–IV → VII–III → VI–II → V–I). The sequential version has unmistakable forward momentum; the plain version is harmonically fine but inert by comparison.
ex2 — the descending-fifths sequence through all seven diatonic degrees: I–IV–VII–III–VI–II–V–I. Every root motion is a descending fifth (or ascending fourth), which Piston identifies as the strongest root progression. This is the Baroque "circle of fifths" sequence and the harmonic engine behind a vast amount of common-practice music. Absolutely worth knowing in your bones.
ex3 — ascending-seconds sequence: pattern I–V transposed up by a step each time (I–V → IIm–VI → IIIm–VII). The diatonic variation at each step (major, minor, then minor-diminished) is exactly the "tonal sequence" irregularity Piston describes — it preserves the key while the pattern changes subtly.
ex4 — secondary dominant sequence: plain VI–V–IV compared to VofVI–VI → VofV–V → VofIV–IV. The secondary dominant version is harmonically far richer, each pair bound tightly by the dominant-tonic relationship, chromatic notes arriving and resolving at each step.
ex5 — the complete circle-of-fifths secondary dominant chain (Ch. 14 revisited as a sequence): VofIII → VofVI → VofII → VofV → V → I. Six consecutive dominant resolutions before the final tonic. The accumulated chromatic tension and release is one of the most satisfying single passages in tonal harmony.
ex6 — modulating sequence through three keys by ascending fifths: C→G→D, using II–V as the repeating pattern at each level. The pivot chord at each junction is the final chord of one pattern statement becoming the first chord of the next. This is the standard modulating sequence.
ex7 — chromatic modulating sequence descending by major thirds: C→Ab→E. The key relationships are distant (not in any common key family), giving the passage a floating, tonally ambiguous quality before each new tonic arrives. This is the late-Romantic modulating sequence sound.
ex8 — the complete musical context: establish C major, then the secondary dominant sequence as development, then break the sequence with the II6–I64–V7–I cadential formula. The sequence drives; the cadence arrives. This is the model for how to use the sequence in FormaComposition: two bars of establishment, three pattern-pairs of sequential development, then the cadential formula. Done.
