# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 16
## The Diminished Seventh Chord

> *A chord made up entirely of tendency tones would seem to be a chord of very definite tonal significance, but, paradoxically, the diminished seventh chord is the most ambiguous of chords.*

---

## Origin: Incomplete Dominant Minor Ninth

Adding a third above the dominant seventh creates a **dominant ninth chord**. Two forms:
- **V9** (major ninth) = G B D F A in C major
- **V9b** (minor ninth) = G B D F Ab in C major

Dominant ninth chords are most often found with the **root omitted**. The incomplete dominant minor ninth — leading-tone triad plus diminished seventh — is the **diminished seventh chord**.

The full group of dominant harmony, from simplest to most complex:
`V` — `V7` — `V°` (leading-tone triad) — `V9` — `V°9` (dim7) — and their inversions

The **diminished seventh chord** (V°9) is by far the commonest of the dominant ninth family.

---

## Structure

In C major, V°9 on the leading-tone B:

```
B — D — F — Ab
```

Intervals from root: minor 3rd, diminished 5th, diminished 7th.  
Equivalently: **four stacked minor thirds** (under equal temperament).

Every factor is a tendency tone:
- **B** (leading-tone) → up to C
- **D** (second degree, the ninth) → down
- **F** (subdominant, the seventh) → down
- **Ab** (minor sixth degree, the diminished seventh) → down

Every interval between any two factors is dissonant — three diminished fifths and three diminished sevenths (or their inversions). This chord is pure dissonance.

---

## The Central Paradox: Most Ambiguous Chord

A chord entirely of tendency tones should define the key with maximum clarity. Yet:

> *"The diminished seventh chord is the most ambiguous of chords."*

Because: **all thirds are minor thirds** — the chord divides the octave into four equal parts. The inversion of the diminished seventh is an augmented second, which under equal temperament sounds exactly like a minor third. Therefore:

- All four inversions sound **harmonically identical**
- The chord and all its inversions have **the same sound**
- The leading-tone cannot be identified by ear alone — only by resolution

---

## Enharmonic Equivalents

One diminished seventh chord can be written in **four different spellings**, each designating a different tone as the leading-tone and therefore implying a different key:

```
B  D  F  Ab  →  V°9 in C (leading-tone = B)
D  F  Ab Cb  →  V°9 in Eb (leading-tone = D)
F  Ab Cb Ebb →  V°9 in Gb (leading-tone = F)
Ab Cb Ebb Gbb→  V°9 in A (leading-tone = Ab = G#)
```

The four "roots" (a major third below each leading-tone) form the arpeggio of another diminished seventh chord. In equal temperament: only **three distinct diminished seventh chords** exist acoustically, covering all twelve keys — one of the most remarkable properties of equal temperament.

Without enharmonic change, one chord serves as the dominant of **four different keys**. With enharmonic changes, **twelve** interpretations become available per chord — and multiplied by the four spellings: **forty-eight possible interpretations** of one chord.

---

## Regular Resolution

**Both diminished fifths contract to thirds** in resolution, without regard to the resulting doubling. If a diminished fifth is inverted (appearing as an augmented fourth), it expands to a sixth.

The chord resolves with equal ease to **major or minor tonic**.

### Voice Leading Freedom

Irregularity of voice leading is **common practice** in the treatment of the diminished seventh chord. Voices are allowed to move freely — through augmented seconds, diminished fifths, and other "unmelodic" intervals — because this chord originates in instrumental writing. The fundamental principle of resolving tendency tones applies, but the path through the chord's factors is not restricted.

---

## Inversions

Since the root of the chord is technically absent (the leading-tone is not a root), there is no true root position. The standard notation:

| Position | Symbol | Bass note |
|---|---|---|
| Leading-tone in bass | **V°9** | B (in C major) |
| First "inversion" | V°6/5 | D |
| Second "inversion" | V°4/3 | F |
| Third "inversion" | V°2 | Ab |

These "inversions" all sound alike harmonically under equal temperament. They vary **contrapuntally** — the bass note's direction of resolution differs for each position. The second degree (D) in the bass ordinarily resolves to the **third degree** (E) to avoid a direct fifth.

In four-part writing: **no factor of a diminished seventh chord is ever omitted**.

---

## As Secondary Dominants

The diminished seventh chord functions as a secondary dominant wherever such chords are used. Examples from Piston:

- **V°9ofII** — in C major: C# E G Bb (approaches D minor)
- **V°9ofIV** — in C major: E G# B D (approaches F major)
- **V°9ofV** — in C major: F# A C Eb (approaches G major)
- **V°9ofVI** — in C major: G# B D F (approaches A minor)

### Nondominant Diminished Sevenths

Two special forms that do **not** fall in the category of dominants:
- **II7** with root and third raised (chromatically altered supertonic seventh) — acts as **appoggiatura chord to I**
- **VI7** with root and third raised (chromatically altered submediant seventh) — acts as **appoggiatura chord to V7**

These are identified by their resolutions, not by their notation.

---

## Irregular Resolution

Because it is the **resolution** that establishes the tonal identity of the diminished seventh, most apparently irregular resolutions are actually regular when the notation is reconsidered. Composers — especially those writing for keyboard — have never been scrupulous about the grammatical spelling of this chord.

> *"The student must be prepared to judge the chord by what it does rather than by its appearance on paper."*

Harmonic irregularity (wrong root progression) in the diminished seventh is rarer than it appears. Enharmonic reinterpretation usually reveals a regular resolution in a different key.

---

## Consecutive Diminished Sevenths

Sequences of more than two diminished seventh chords create a **momentary uncertainty of tonality** — the vagueness of each chord accumulates. Moving by half step in similar motion gives a chromatic passing-tone effect; parallel movement is disguised by changes of position between the right and left hands (in keyboard writing). In rapid passage, the ear grasps only the **first and last chords** of the group.

---

## Modulation

The diminished seventh's tonal ambiguity becomes a **versatile modulation tool**. Without enharmonic change, one chord serves as V of four keys. With all enharmonic changes: twelve interpretations. This makes it the most powerful single pivot chord available, though with important limitations:

**Best practice:** Use the **chord of resolution** (the chord after the dim7) as the actual pivot chord, not the dim7 itself. The chord before the dim7 or the tonic to which it resolves = better common ground between two keys.

Example: modulating from A to G using B°7 (= V°9 in C):
- In A major: I → IV → I → V°9 (approaches V of A)
- Enharmonic change: G-sharp = A-flat → now V°9ofIV in G major → resolves to IV in G → V → I in G

---

## FormaComposition Implications

**The dim7 chord is the single most enharmonically flexible chord.** A single MIDI pitch set (e.g., B D F Ab) can resolve convincingly to any of twelve keys with appropriate enharmonic reinterpretation. For FormaComposition, this means the dim7 is the universal pivot — any section can be connected to any other section by routing through a dim7 chord.

**The dim7 cannot be identified by ear before resolution.** FormaComposition's chord-symbol system needs to specify the intended resolution target when using V°9. Labeling it just as "dim7" is ambiguous — the engine needs to know which leading-tone is intended (B°7 vs D°7 vs F°7 vs Ab°7 are acoustically identical but harmonically different).

**No factor of the dim7 is ever omitted.** Unlike V7 where the fifth may be dropped, the dim7 always has all four notes. A three-note voicing of a dim7 is a diminished triad (VII) — a different chord.

**Consecutive diminished sevenths by half-step = tonal suspension.** A passage of two or three dim7 chords moving by semitone creates a momentary floating quality — tonality is suspended. This maps directly to transition passages between sections in FormaComposition, where tonal ambiguity is sometimes a desired texture effect before arriving at the new key.

**Voice leading irregularities are acceptable in dim7 chords.** The engine should not apply standard voice leading constraints (avoid augmented seconds, etc.) as strictly to dim7 chords as to triads. Augmented seconds are characteristic of this chord's motion.

**The dim7 as secondary dominant is available on every scale degree.** V°9ofII, V°9ofIV, V°9ofV, V°9ofVI are all available in major and minor. Each introduces four new chromatic tones (one of the four factors will be non-diatonic in the home key). The harmonic color is intense; use deliberately.
