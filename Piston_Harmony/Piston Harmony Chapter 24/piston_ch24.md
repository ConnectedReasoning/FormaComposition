# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 24
## Augmented Sixth Chords

> *The interval of the augmented sixth expands in its normal resolution to an octave which is the octave on the dominant.*

---

## What Augmented Sixth Chords Are

The **augmented sixth chord group** consists of four related chords sharing a single characteristic interval: the **augmented sixth** formed between the **minor sixth degree** (Ab in C major/minor) in the bass and the **raised fourth degree** (F# in C) in an upper voice.

This interval — Ab to F# — is an augmented sixth (ten semitones). It resolves by **expanding outward to an octave**: Ab descends a half step to G, and F# ascends a half step to G. Both tendency tones converge on G — the **dominant**.

> *"The augmented sixth chords are nondominant in function. They are strongly tonal since they indicate unmistakably the dominant of the key."*

The chord does **not** come from a dominant with lowered fifth — it comes from a **subdominant with raised root**. The contrapuntal origin shows the raised fourth degree as a chromatic passing or leading tone moving toward the dominant.

---

## The Four Forms

All four augmented sixth chords share:
- **Ab in the bass** (minor 6th degree, or ♭6̂)
- **F# in an upper voice** (raised 4th degree, or ♯4̂)
- **C (tonic)** in another voice

The **fourth voice** distinguishes the four forms:

| Symbol | 4th voice | Interval structure | Historical name |
|---|---|---|---|
| **IV⁶♯** (form *a*) | C only (doubled tonic) | Aug 6th | "Italian sixth" |
| **IV⁶♯₅** (form *b*) | Eb (minor 3rd degree) | Aug 6th + perfect 5th | "German sixth" |
| **II⁶♯₃** (form *c*) | D (natural 2nd degree) | Aug 6th + aug 4th | "French sixth" |
| **II⁶♯₃** (form *d*) | D# (raised 2nd degree) | Aug 6th + doubly aug 4th | "Doubly augmented fourth" |

**Note on the names:** "Italian," "German," and "French" sixth are used by many theorists but are **not universally accepted** and have nothing like the established usage of "Neapolitan sixth." Piston identifies form *c* as the "French" (= augmented six-four-three, built on II rather than IV) and form *b* as the "German" (= augmented six-five-three, with the minor third degree).

Form *b* and *d* sound like dominant seventh chords (enharmonically); form *c* is distinguished by having the natural second degree (D) rather than the minor third (Eb).

---

## Resolution

**Regular resolution: IV⁶♯ → V (or V preceded by I64)**

Voice movements:
- **Ab (bass)** descends by half step → **G** (dominant)
- **F# (upper voice)** ascends by half step → **G** (dominant) — to the octave above
- **C (tonic)** moves down by step → **B** (leading-tone in V, or held as suspension)
- **4th voice** moves according to its identity

The **raised fourth degree (F#) and the minor sixth degree (Ab) must not be doubled** — both are tendency tones that must resolve in one direction. The tonic (C) is the only safe tone to double in the plain augmented sixth (*a*).

The resolution creates **parallel octaves on G** between the bass and the voice that has F# — both arrive on G simultaneously. These parallel octaves are **practiced and accepted** except when occurring between soprano and bass. They are most often found between tenor and bass.

### Via I64

Very commonly: **IV⁶♯ → I64 → V**. The cadential six-four acts as an intermediate step, letting the raised fourth degree descend chromatically to the dominant through the tonic appoggiatura, making the parallel octaves unnoticeable.

---

## Form *a*: The Augmented Sixth (Plain)

**Ab C F#** (3 distinct tones — tonic doubled):
```
F#    (aug 4 above bass)
C     (minor 3rd above bass)
C     (doubled)
Ab    (bass)
```
The fourth voice steps up by step to the dominant (or to I64). Simple, unambiguous. The doubled tonic moves toward the leading-tone in V.

---

## Form *b*: The Augmented Six-Five-Three (German Sixth)

**Ab C Eb F#** (adds the minor third degree):
```
F#    (aug 6th above bass)
Eb    (minor 3rd degree — perfect 5th above bass)
C     (minor 3rd above bass)
Ab    (bass)
```
The Eb (third degree) is tied over as a suspension or repeated as an appoggiatura before descending to D (second degree) of the dominant chord. This **avoids parallel fifths** between the Eb and Bb... wait — actually parallel fifths arise between the Eb→D and Ab→G motion, and these fifths are generally **practiced** (occurring between inner voices) except when between soprano and bass.

When followed by a dominant seventh chord, the **raised fourth degree descends chromatically** (F#→F♮) rather than ascending — an irregular resolution in the manner of an irregular leading-tone. This sounds like a succession of dominant seventh chords (Ab7 → Eb7 = enharmonic).

**Strongly indicates the minor mode**, since it contains both minor third (Eb) and minor sixth (Ab) degrees.

---

## Form *c*: The Augmented Six-Four-Three (French Sixth)

**Ab C D F#** (adds the natural second degree):
```
F#    (aug 6th above bass)
D     (perfect 4th above bass — the raised "aug 4th" = doubly aug 4th in d)
C     (minor 3rd above bass)
Ab    (bass)
```
Built on **root II** rather than IV. The presence of the natural second degree (D) provides a common tone with the dominant chord, which will usually be repeated or tied over in the resolution.

Employed with both major and minor mode characteristics — contributes to the impression of **interchangeability of modes**. Very common preceding the cadential six-four chord. Its tonal clarity is especially useful after a modulation.

---

## Form *d*: The Doubly Augmented Fourth

**Ab C D# F#** (adds the raised second degree):
- The distinguishing factor: **D#** (raised second degree) instead of D natural
- This implies a resolution to the **major third degree** (E♮)
- The interval between Ab (bass) and D# is a **doubly augmented fourth** — enharmonically equivalent to a perfect fifth
- Indistinguishable from form *b* by sound alone; only its resolution reveals which it is
- Composers frequently write it incorrectly as form *b* — only performance context clarifies

---

## Irregular Resolutions

Harmonically, augmented sixth chords have **few irregular resolutions**. Mostly they progress to tonic harmony (rather than dominant). Notable:
- Form *b* resolving to **I root position** — the augmented sixth interval resolves as a direct fifth, accepted in this context as a characteristic feature of César Franck's style
- Augmented sixths appearing as **dissonant chords over dominant pedal** — strikingly effective
- Chords on roots other than II and IV — when this occurs, the relationship is more accurately described by the **extension of the secondary dominant principle** (the augmented sixth standing as a chromatic IV or II of a secondary tonal area)

---

## Inversions

When a factor other than the sixth degree is in the bass, the chord retains its identity as an augmented sixth, though the characteristic interval becomes less prominent (appearing as a diminished third in inversion). The identity is preserved through resolution rather than interval structure.

The chord may also appear over a **tonic or third degree pedal** as a double auxiliary or double appoggiatura effect.

---

## Modulation

As a pivot chord, the augmented sixth is most often used for its **enharmonic similarity to a dominant seventh chord**. Form *b* (German sixth) in C minor = Ab C Eb F# = sounds exactly like Eb dominant seventh (Eb G Bb Db enharmonically). When the raised fourth degree is reinterpreted as a minor seventh, the chord becomes the V7 of a new key **a half-step away**.

This is the mechanism of **half-step modulations** in the Romantic period — the German sixth (or form *b* augmented sixth) becomes the dominant seventh of the new key. Since the minor sixth degree = dominant of the new key, the two keys involved will always be **a half-step apart**.

---

## The Tristan Chord Connection

The opening chord of Wagner's *Prelude to Tristan und Isolde* (Ex. 548) is labeled by Piston as an augmented six-four-three chord (form *c*) on root II — the "French sixth" configuration. The appoggiatura forming a diminished octave with the raised fourth degree (F# against F♮) is "of fairly frequent occurrence" according to Piston. This places the Tristan chord firmly within the augmented sixth vocabulary.

## FormaComposition Implications

**The augmented sixth chord is the most directionally specific pre-dominant chord available.** Where II6 or N6 approach the dominant smoothly, the augmented sixth approaches it with maximum chromatic urgency — both tendency tones pressing outward toward the same goal (G in C major). It creates the strongest possible dominant arrival.

**IV⁶♯ → I64 → V7 → I is the supreme cadential formula.** The German or French sixth before the cadential six-four before the dominant seventh before the tonic — this is the fullest possible cadential approach in tonal music. FormaComposition should know this as a named four-chord sequence.

**The German sixth (form *b*) sounds like a dominant seventh chord.** When you hear Ab C Eb F# in C, you also hear it as an Eb major dominant seventh. This enharmonic ambiguity is the Romantic modulation mechanism. For FormaComposition, this means using a German sixth = using an Eb7 chord with a specific resolution toward G (not Eb) — the interpretation is determined by what follows, not by the chord itself.

**These chords are nondominant in function but dominant in direction.** They are fundamentally subdominant-family chords (built on ♭6̂ and ♯4̂) that have been chromatically altered to point with maximum intensity toward V. They are not secondary dominants; they are altered subdominant chords. The distinction matters for voice leading — their tendency tones (♭6̂ and ♯4̂) resolve outward, not inward as dominant seventh tendency tones do.
