ex1 — the mechanism stripped bare: just the two tendency tones (Ab and F#) sounding together as an augmented sixth, then expanding outward to the same G an octave apart. This is the chapter's entire logic in two chords. Then form (a) — the plain augmented sixth — in full four-part context.
ex2 — all four forms back to back: (a) Italian/plain, (b) German (+Eb), (c) French (+D natural), (d) Doubly augmented (+D#). Forms b and d are enharmonically identical in equal temperament — only their resolution would distinguish them in practice. The French sixth (c) sounds noticeably more open because of the D natural rather than Eb.
ex3 — the complete cadential formula in both versions: German sixth → V7 → I (direct, with the parallel octaves on G), then the polished version German sixth → I64 → V7 → I. The I64 absorbs the parallel octaves and makes the approach smoother. This is the supreme cadential formula.
ex4 — N6 versus augmented sixth in identical contexts. The N6 has a dark, subdominant shadow quality; the augmented sixth has a more urgently chromatic, outward-pressing quality. Both resolve to the dominant but with different emotional characters.
ex5 — the enharmonic modulation: German sixth in C minor (Ab C Eb F#) first resolves normally to C minor V→I, then the same chord resolves as Eb dominant seventh → Ab major. Same four pitches, two completely different tonal destinations — one arriving on G, one on Ab. This is the half-step modulation mechanism of the Romantic period.
ex6 — the French sixth (form c) in slow, sustained context — the chord-type Piston identifies in the Tristan Prelude. The Ab C D F# sonority has an open, unresolved quality that makes it ideal for sustained, atmospheric passages.
ex7 — augmented sixth over a dominant pedal: G bass held, then Ab C F# sounding above it. The Ab against the G is a minor second — maximum dissonance. The chord is "strikingly effective" as Piston says; even on general MIDI instruments the sharpness of this combination is audible.
ex8 — the complete passage in both modes: German sixth → I64 → V7 → I in C minor, then French sixth → I64 → V7 → I in C major (with the minor IV for color). The switch from minor to major cadence with the same augmented sixth type demonstrates the chord's modal flexibility.
