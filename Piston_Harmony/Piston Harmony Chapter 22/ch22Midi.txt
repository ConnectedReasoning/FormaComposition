Chapter 1: Scales and Intervals
The core claim: The interval is the fundamental unit of harmony — not the chord, not the scale. Everything else is built from pairs of notes and their relationships.
Scales Piston recognizes (three only)

Major — the baseline reference. All intervals are named relative to major scale degrees.
Harmonic minor — raised VII, creating the leading-tone pull. The characteristic augmented second between VI and VII is a harmonic feature, not a melodic one.
Melodic minor — raised VI and VII ascending to smooth that augmented second; reverts to natural descending. Piston's observation: major and minor scales differ only in their III and VI degrees — everything else is identical. This is a useful simplification.
Chromatic scale — treated as a variant/alteration of the other two, not an independent system.

(No modes discussed here — those come in Ch. 4, Tonality and Modality.)
Scale degree names (these matter for FormaComposition)
RomanNameNotesITonicKey centerIISupertonicOne step above tonicIIIMediantHalfway tonic→dominantIVSubdominantAs far below tonic as V is aboveVDominant"Actually a dominant element in the key"VISubmediantHalfway down tonic→subdominantVIILeading-toneStrong melodic pull toward tonic
These names are the vocabulary for harmonic function — directly what your progression field encodes as Roman numerals.
Interval classification
Two layers:

General name — count lines and spaces between the two notes: second, third, fourth... ninth.
Specific quality — measure against the major scale built on the lower note:

Coincides with major scale → major (or perfect for unisons, 4ths, 5ths, octaves)
Half-step smaller than major → minor
Half-step larger than major/perfect → augmented
Half-step smaller than minor/perfect → diminished



Compound intervals: Anything beyond an octave. Piston notes the ninth is a "characteristic feature of certain harmonic forms" — meaning it carries its own harmonic identity, not just an octave-transposed second. This matters for your ninth/eleventh chord chapters later.
Consonance vs. Dissonance — the key passage
Piston draws a hard line:

Consonant: perfect intervals + major/minor thirds and sixths
Dissonant: augmented/diminished intervals + seconds, sevenths, ninths
(Exception: perfect fourth is dissonant when there's no third or fifth below it)

Then he says something directly relevant to FormaComposition:

"Music without dissonant intervals is often lifeless and negative, since it is the dissonant element which furnishes much of the sense of movement and rhythmic energy."

This is the theoretical grounding for why soft pads holding consonant whole notes — your "texture rut" — produce lifeless output. It's not just an aesthetic preference; Piston is saying dissonance is structurally necessary for energy. The engine needs to allow it, not just permit it accidentally.
Inversion of intervals
Three types — only one of which matters much for your work:

True (mirror) inversion — rare, academic
Contrapuntal inversion — same scale, voices swap. Common. When a major 6th inverts contrapuntally, the general name stays "sixth" but the specific name may change.
Harmonic inversion — lower note becomes upper (or vice versa), both general and specific names usually change. A major 6th inverts to a minor 3rd.

This is the foundation for chord inversions in Ch. 2, and ultimately for your bass voice logic — when the bass plays the third or fifth of a chord instead of the root, it's harmonic inversion in Piston's terms.
Enharmonic intervals
Same sound, different spelling — augmented second vs. minor third, for example. Piston flags that they're distinguishable in harmonic context even when they sound identical in isolation. This is why the engine's Roman numeral system matters: bVII and #VI might produce the same MIDI note but mean completely different things harmonically.

What this chapter gives FormaComposition directly:
The consonance/dissonance table is essentially a tension map. Any time your arc or density controls need to be more precise, this is the foundation — consonant intervals = stable/resolved, dissonant intervals = energy/movement. The leading-tone (VII) wanting to resolve to tonic is the strongest single force in tonal harmony and the reason dominant chords are so powerful. Ch. 13 (Dominant Seventh) will go deep on that.
