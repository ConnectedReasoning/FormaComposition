# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 15
## Irregular Resolutions

> *A regular resolution may be made with extreme irregularity of melodic movement, whereas an irregular resolution may show strict regularity of voice leading.*

---

## Two Kinds of Irregularity

The regular resolution of the dominant seventh chord is to the tonic triad. All other resolutions are **irregular**. But the term covers two fundamentally different things:

1. **Voice leading irregularity** — The root progression is normal (V→I), but the individual voices don't follow their natural tendencies. The leading-tone may not rise; the seventh may not descend.

2. **Harmonic irregularity** — The root itself goes somewhere other than the tonic. The chord after V7 is not I.

This distinction matters because a progressiom may be irregular in one sense while perfectly regular in the other. All chord progressions are products of melodic movement; the "vocabulary of chords" exists as a useful analytical framework, but the voices move contrapuntally first and harmonic labels follow.

---

## V7 → VI: The Deceptive Cadence (review)

Already established in Ch. 3 and 12. In V7 form:
- The leading-tone (B) moves up to the tonic (C) — same as regular resolution
- The seventh (F) descends — same as regular
- The fifth (D) descends to E (third of VI, which must be doubled)
- The doubled root (G in an incomplete V7) must move up by skip to the tonic

**Include the fifth in V7 when planning V7–VI.** The incomplete V7 (root doubled, no fifth) doesn't connect smoothly with VI. The complete V7 (with D) gives the fifth a natural path to the third of VI.

---

## V7 → V7ofIV: Chromatic Resolution

A dominant seventh resolving to another dominant seventh — the chord that would function as VofIV. The minor seventh above the tonic (Bb, = subdominant of IV) is in chromatic relationship with the leading-tone (B natural). To avoid the cross-relation, the leading-tone usually **descends** — an exception to its normal upward tendency.

```
V7:     G  B  D  F
V7ofIV: C  E  G  Bb  (= VofIV, the I with minor 7th)
```

The progression G→C, D→E, F→Bb... the B leading-tone descends to Bb rather than rising to C, avoiding the harsh B–Bb cross-relation.

When two seventh chords occur in succession, one of them is usually incomplete (root doubled, no fifth). This is the natural result of moving voices to nearest positions.

**V2ofIV:** A particularly effective variant with the seventh of V (= F) in the bass. The chromatic bass makes the progression especially striking.

---

## V7 → II: Resolution to the Supertonic

A weak resolution — II is structurally similar to a dominant ninth chord minus its root and third. The seventh of V7 (F) is common to both V7 and II (D–F–A–C), so it **does not resolve** across this progression. This reduces the sense of resolution.

A more satisfactory approach: **V° → VofV** (the leading-tone triad without root — effectively the upper three notes of V7 — resolving to the dominant of V). The seventh can then move upward chromatically (as C#), since it has been reinterpreted as the leading-tone of V.

---

## V7 → III: Resolution to the Mediant

Resolution to the **major third degree** (III, not III minor) is rarely found. The voice leading produces a doubled third in the chord of resolution.

More common and more musically natural: V7 resolves to the form of III that functions as **VofVI** — the dominant of the submediant. This is a well-disguised irregular resolution because the chord of arrival looks like an E major chord (in C major), but it is tonally understood as the dominant of A minor, and the progression continues to VI.

---

## V7 → IV: Resolution to the Subdominant

In this progression the seventh of V7 (F) may be prolonged — **held or repeated** — into the IV chord, since F is present in both. The leading-tone, because of the tritone cross-relation it would cause if placed in an outer voice, is **placed in an inside voice** (alto or tenor).

When both chords are in root position this constraint on voice placement is important. More often IV appears in **first inversion** (IV6), which gives more freedom for voice placement and allows either the tonic or the fourth degree to be doubled.

---

## V7 → V7ofIV (Chain): A Powerful Progression

The succession V7 → V7ofIV is followed by V7ofIV resolving to IV. This creates a two-step approach to the subdominant using chromatic enrichment. The second chord already "belongs to" the subdominant key as its dominant seventh. Piston singles this out as among the best examples of irregular resolution in support of tonality — the resolution of V7ofVI to IV supports the **tonic** better than its regular resolution (to VI) by virtue of IV's greater tonal importance.

---

## Secondary Dominants and Irregular Resolution

The practice of irregular resolution extends to secondary dominants, though in somewhat lesser scope. When a secondary dominant fails to resolve to its expected temporary tonic, the progression is irregular.

**Key principle:** If the chord of resolution (after the irregular progression) is **unquestionably in the main key**, the secondary dominant is still understood as such without weakening the tonality. The tonal clarity of the resolution chord validates the secondary dominant, even though its expected temporary tonic never arrived.

**V7ofVI → IV** is the outstanding example:
- V7ofVI's expected resolution = VI (A minor in C major)
- Its actual resolution = IV (F major)
- This is irregular — but IV is a strong tonal degree, and the progression E–G#–B–D → F–A–C makes harmonic sense

The analysis: in the temporary key of A minor, V7ofVI → IV is like V7 → bVI in A minor — a deceptive cadence in the temporary key. The secondary dominant's tonal function is understood even without its normal resolution.

Similarly:
- **V7ofIII → I** (the VofIII resolves to tonic rather than III) — often analyzable as an appoggiatura chord to I
- **V7ofV → III** — an irregular resolution within the chain of dominants

---

## Dissonant Chord to Dissonant Chord

> *"These two chords also show a dissonant chord resolving to another dissonant chord, an effect of frequent occurrence."*

One of the dissonant intervals may resolve while the other moves in parallel to an equivalent dissonance. In V7 → V7ofIV: the minor seventh resolves normally (F stays / moves to Bb), while the diminished fifth moves **in parallel motion** to another diminished fifth (or augmented fourth). This is perfectly acceptable — the critical observation is to notice how the dissonant intervals progress in each succession of dissonant chords.

---

## The Formulae

Key patterns from Piston's formulae pages to internalize:

- `V7–VI` (deceptive, complete V7 preferred)
- `V7–IV` / `V7–IV6` (subdominant resolution, leading-tone inside)
- `V7–V7ofIV–IV` (chromatic path to subdominant)
- `V7–II7` (weak, seventh held)
- `V2–III` (third inversion to mediant — bass descends)
- `V7ofVI–IV` (irregular secondary → tonal degree)
- `V7ofV–V–I` followed by `V7ofV–III` for comparison

## FormaComposition Implications

**Irregular resolutions are the "unexpected moment" every phrase needs (Ch. 7).** The V7–IV progression, V7–VI deceptive cadence, and V7ofVI→IV are all specific, named instances of Piston's requirement that every harmonization include "some aspect which is not entirely commonplace and expected."

**V7→IV is available without any chromatic notes in C major.** V7 (G B D F) → IV (F A C) — both are purely diatonic in C major. This makes it the most accessible irregular resolution: no accidentals required, strong harmonic surprise. The leading-tone (B) just needs to be in an inner voice.

**V7ofVI→IV is the single most useful irregular secondary dominant progression.** It confirms the tonic area (IV is strongly tonal), introduces the raised G# from VofVI as harmonic color, and avoids the somewhat bland V7ofVI→VI progression. Worth adding as a named pattern to FormaComposition's progression library.

**V7→VofIV→IV creates a chromatic approach to the subdominant** that is one of the most effective ways to end a major-key piece with subdominant emphasis. The three-chord pattern produces a striking Bb in the middle chord (in C major) that warmly prepares the F major arrival. This is the "late 19th century" ending sound.

**Dissonant chord to dissonant chord is valid and frequent.** The engine should not require every V7 to resolve to a triad. Two seventh chords in succession (e.g., V7→V7ofIV, or V7→II7) are musically correct. The rule is that within the pair, one is usually incomplete; the voice leading still follows each chord's tendency tones.
