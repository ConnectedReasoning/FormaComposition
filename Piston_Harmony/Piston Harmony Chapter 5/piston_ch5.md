# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 5
## Harmonic Rhythm

> *For vitality of contrapuntal texture over a clear harmonic rhythmic background, the works of J. S. Bach remain the models of perfection throughout the period.*

---

## Meter vs. Rhythm — The Distinction That Matters

**Meter** is simply measure. Bar lines. Regular subdivision. **Meter has no rhythm.** It is a container, not a content. The common error is treating the first beat as automatically strong because it is the first beat — but the first beat is strong *only when the music calls for it*.

The 18th–19th century common-practice period was also the period of the greatest "tyranny of the bar-line" — dance forms instilled the idea of regular symmetrical pulse into all music. Composers of earlier centuries worked with free, prose-like rhythms. The 20th century tried to recapture that freedom.

The practical consequence: strong and weak beats must be earned by the music, not assumed from the time signature.

---

## Rhythmic Texture: Two Independent Streams

The rhythm of music in its total effect derives from **two main sources**:

| Source | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Melodic rhythm** | The rhythm of the individual voices — each voice has its own pattern of note values |
| **Harmonic rhythm** | The rhythm of the *root changes* — when does the harmony change, and how strong is each change |

Percussion underlies both but is not itself a rhythmic source in this analysis — its role is to heighten or underline melodic or harmonic rhythm, not replace it.

These two streams are **independent**. The Beethoven Op.31 No.3 example shows four melodic lines each with different rhythmic patterns, all disagreeing with each other and with the meter — yet the harmonic rhythm is distinct from all of them and reads as its own pattern.

> *"The root changes, which give the rhythmic pattern of the harmony, are not regular in time... Both frequency of change and quality of that change must receive attention."*

---

## The Two Dimensions of Harmonic Rhythm

### 1. Frequency of Change

How often does the harmony change?

- **Too frequent:** Restlessness and bustle. Every beat a new chord = exhausting.
- **Widely spaced:** Breadth and relaxation. Long durations on single harmonies = stillness, stability.
- **Varied:** The norm. Harmonic rhythm that changes its own pace creates shape and phrase structure.

The **harmonic meter** (one chord per bar? two? four?) is a compositional parameter as fundamental as tempo. The Scarlatti Presto example has harmonic meter of one chord per measure — the fast tempo makes one-per-bar feel right. The Haydn Adagio example has the same root progressions but slow tempo forces each chord to carry harmonic weight.

### 2. Quality of Change — Strong vs. Weak Progressions

Every root movement has an **inherent rhythmic value**:

| Root motion | Rhythmic quality | Analogy |
|---|---|---|
| Down a 5th / up a 4th | **Strong** — weak to strong | Bar-line arrives on the second chord |
| By step (2nd) | **Strong** — weak to strong | Bar-line arrives on the second chord |
| Up a 3rd | **Weak** — strong to weak | Bar-line arrives on the *first* chord |
| Down a 3rd | **Ambiguous** — not definitely either |

The strong progression "gives the impression of weak (light) proceeding to strong (heavy)." The bar-line can properly be placed *between* two chords of a strong progression — the first chord is upbeat, the second is downbeat.

**A strong progression can become rhythmically weak by association.** When a strong progression is surrounded by other strong progressions, the ear groups them and the individual strong progression loses its accent within the group. Rhythmic hierarchy above the beat level emerges.

## Static Harmony

Complete absence of harmonic rhythm. Wagner's *Das Rheingold* prelude: 136 bars of E-flat major. Not a single root change.

Static harmony is:
- **Intentional and effective** when the composer uses it as a specific dramatic or atmospheric resource
- **A defect** when it results from the composer's indifference or unawareness

> *"Like all other technical resources, it is successful when appropriate, and is bound to be ineffective when the result of indifference or unawareness."*

This is the theoretical grounding for the soft-pad texture rut problem in generative music. Harmony that never changes doesn't have a technical flaw — it has a *dramatic* flaw. The question is always whether the stasis is serving a purpose or just happening by default.

## Other Rhythmic Factors

### Time Values (Agogic Accent)
Long note values = heavy and strong. Short values = light and weak. This applies to chords: a chord held for two bars feels heavier than one held for a quarter beat. The agogic (time-value) accent can confirm or override the metric accent.

### Dissonance
Dissonant chords and their resolutions are contrapuntal in principle — they are an element of motion. A dissonant chord moving to resolution is a rhythmic event. But crucially: dissonance can compose either a strong *or* a weak progression depending on the root motion involved. The harmonic rhythm is concerned with root movement; the dissonance is the quality of the superstructure above it.

### Dynamic Indications
*Crescendo, diminuendo, sforzando*, accents — these confirm and accentuate the natural rhythmic feeling already present, though they can deliberately contradict it for expressive effect. Dynamics do not create harmonic rhythm; they reveal or distort it.

### Passing Chords
Some vertical combinations that look like independent chords are actually passing tones in all voices happening to harmonize momentarily. The test is rhythmic: is the chord in a rhythmically accented position? Does the musical pace give it time to register as a harmonic event? Fast tempo = passing chord. Slow tempo = independent harmony. The distinction depends on context, not notation.

### Pedal Points
A sustained bass note (or occasionally an inner or upper voice) held through harmonies foreign to it. Effect: **rhythmically static** — the bass deprives the progressions above it of their root and makes them sound like melodic tones over a single, unchanging root. Harmonic rhythm above a pedal is weakened or suspended.

## Syncopation

Syncopation requires a well-established rhythmic pulse to dislocate. It gives a strong accent where one is not expected and suppresses the normal accent of the pulse. Four types:

1. **Melody syncopated against harmonic rhythm / pulse** — the moving line contradicts the established pulse (Schubert Quartet Op.29 example)
2. **Pulse implied but not actually present** — the ear maintains an absent pulse by analogy with preceding measures (Beethoven Op.101)
3. **Both melodic and harmonic rhythms syncopated** against an implied pulse
4. **Harmonic rhythm syncopated while melody agrees with pulse** — harmony changes on the weak beat while melody lands on the strong beat (Chopin Prelude Op.28 No.4)

Type 4 is the Chopin E-minor Prelude model — melody in long notes on the beat, harmony shifting constantly on weak positions. This creates the characteristic floating, off-kilter quality.

---

## The Anacrusis (Upbeat)

The anacrusis is the up-beat — notes that precede the first stressed beat of a phrase. Common term is "up-beat" but this is misleading: the up-beat is *not automatically weak*. It may be strong, introducing a weak down-beat. The Brahms Intermezzo Op.76 No.6 example demonstrates a strong anacrusis leading to a weak downbeat.

The rhythmic relationship between anacrusis and thesis (downbeat) is an independent musical parameter from the metric weight of the position.

---

## Texture Types

**Homophonic:** One melody dominates; all other voices subordinate. The accompaniment is frequently "lacking in rhythmic interest, to avoid lessening the prominence of the melody" (Chopin Nocturne model). The harmonic rhythm is carried by the accompaniment.

**Polyphonic:** Two or more independent melodic lines heard simultaneously. The rhythmic independence of the lines is the test of good counterpoint. Harmonic rhythm here emerges from the interaction of the voices — it is implied by the coincidence of the lines, not stated explicitly by block chords.

Bach is named as the model of polyphonic vitality over clear harmonic rhythm. The "obliteration of harmonic progression" in late 19th century chromatic writing — trying to increase contrapuntal interest by obscuring the harmonic rhythm — produced "an impoverishment of the rhythmic life of the music."

## FormaComposition Implications

**Harmonic rhythm is a first-class compositional parameter, equal to which chords you choose.** The `harmony_pattern` field you've already built is the direct implementation of this chapter. Piston is explicit: frequency of change and quality of change are the two dimensions. Both need deliberate control.

**The `harmony_pattern` field encodes the agogic accent.** A chord given 3 beats is heavier than a chord given 1 beat. The pattern `[3, 1, 2, 2]` over 8 beats is not just a rhythm — it assigns weight to the harmonic events. The first chord is heavy; the second is a passing accent; the third and fourth are moderate.

**Static harmony is valid, not wrong — but it must be intentional.** The Wagner example is 136 bars. The FormaComposition texture rut (pads + no harmonic motion) is exactly what Piston calls "the result of indifference or unawareness." The fix is not to avoid static sections but to make them deliberate choices with a purpose — a drone intro, a suspended moment before a resolution — not a default.

**Strong progressions (down 5th, by step) have inherent rhythmic arrival on the second chord.** This means the harmonic rhythm pattern and the progression choice are coupled. If you put a V→I resolution on a weak beat, you're fighting the natural weight of that progression. If you want the resolution to feel like it lands, it should land on a metrically or agogically strong position.

**Weak progressions (up a 3rd) have their weight on the *first* chord.** I→III has the accent on I. The III feels like a departure, not an arrival. Placing I→III across a bar-line (I on beat 4, III on beat 1) contradicts the natural rhythmic character of that progression.

**The Chopin syncopation model is directly applicable.** Melody on the beat + harmony shifting on weak positions = the floating quality of much ambient/new-age music. If FormaComposition's harmony voice starts its chord changes on weak beats while the melody lands on strong ones, this is not an error — it is a named, intentional technique. The `harmony_pattern` onset positions relative to the melody onsets control this.

**Pedal points suppress harmonic rhythm.** A sustained bass note in FormaComposition's bass voice, while harmony changes above, is statically rhythmic by design. This is a valid texture — not the engine failing to resolve the bass. The drone bass is a pedal point and should be understood as one.

**The accompaniment should be "lacking in rhythmic interest" in homophonic texture.** This validates simple, regular `harmony_pattern` values (e.g., on-the-beat whole or half-note block chords) when a melody is dominant. The harmony does not need to be rhythmically interesting when the melody is carrying that role.
