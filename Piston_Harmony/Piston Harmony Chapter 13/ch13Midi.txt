ex1 — the resolution anatomy: first just the two dissonant voices (B and F) sounding together as a diminished fifth, then contracting inward to E and C. Then the full V7 resolving. This makes visible what's happening inside the chord.
ex2 — complete V7 (with fifth D) resolving to incomplete I (standard, three roots one third), versus incomplete V7 (doubled root, no fifth) resolving to complete I. Then V7 resolving to I6 (alternate path). Three versions of the same fundamental progression.
ex3 — V triad versus V7 in identical context. The V without seventh still works; the V7 is noticeably more directed and final. Then the full II6–I64–V7–I formula — hear how the seventh makes the cadence feel inevitable.
ex4 — all four inversions back to back, each preceded by a brief I and each resolving. V7 root → I incomplete; V65 (B bass, leading-tone) → I root; V43 (D bass, passing) → I6; V2 (F bass, strongest) → I6. The bass motion in V2 descending F→E is the most distinctive.
ex5 — V2 versus V7 root position in identical phrase contexts. The descending semitone F→E in the V2 bass is one of the most characteristic bass gestures in tonal music — it sounds "inevitable" in a way the root-position descent doesn't.
ex6 — four preparation grades: (a) suspended seventh tied from IV; (b) repeated tone; (c) by step; (d) by skip (unprepared). Each resolves identically. The suspended version has the most tension before resolution; the skip version arrives most abruptly. Worth loading in Logic with different timbres to hear the stylistic difference clearly.
ex7 — the melodic tritone: ascending F→B continuing to C (leading-tone resolves upward), then descending B→F continuing to E (both tendency tones resolve inward). Two resolutions of the same tritone in opposite directions.
ex8 — a complete phrase that uses all four V7 positions in sequence: V65 (B bass) → I6, then V43 (D bass, passing) → I6, then V2 (F bass, strongest) → I6, then V7 root for the final authentic cadence. This is a realistic demonstration of how V7 in its various forms drives harmonic motion across a full phrase.
