# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 13
## The Dominant Seventh Chord

> *The regular resolution of V₇ to I is probably the most fundamental harmonic progression in music. It is without doubt the commonest, and seems to be felt instinctively as a natural musical word even by an unmusical person.*

---

## The First True Harmonic Dissonance

Seventh chords are built by superposing a third on top of a triad, but they did **not** originate that way. Chords arise from moving voices. The seventh first appeared as a melodic, nonharmonic tone — a passing or suspension tone that happened to sound against the chord below. The dominant seventh is a passing tone that became so characteristic and idiomatic it was absorbed into the harmonic vocabulary as a chord in its own right.

All the dissonances encountered so far (Chs. 1–12) were **contrapuntal dissonances** — tones foreign to the chord, belonging to the horizontal melodic lines. Three exceptions existed: VII, II in minor (diminished triads), and III in minor (augmented triad). The seventh of V₇ is different: it is a **harmonic dissonance** — a dissonance that belongs to the chord itself.

The dominant seventh chord: **G–B–D–F** in C major. Complete figuring: V⁷₅/₃, usually abbreviated to V₇.

---

## Two Dissonant Intervals in V₇

The chord contains two dissonant intervals that define its resolution behavior:

| Interval | Notes (C major) | Resolution tendency |
|---|---|---|
| **Diminished fifth** | B–F (3rd to 7th) | **Contracts** inward — B up by step, F down by step |
| **Minor seventh** | G–F (root to 7th) | Seventh descends one degree |

### The Diminished Fifth (B–F): The Core Tension

The tendency of the diminished fifth is to **contract to a third** — both voices moving inward:
- B (leading-tone, lower note) moves **up** to C (tonic)
- F (subdominant, upper note) moves **down** to E (third of I)

Result: B→C and F→E = the third of I in both voices = tonic chord with doubled third but no fifth (incomplete).

If only B moves up while F holds: C–F = perfect fourth (still dissonant without bass support).

Inversion of the diminished fifth gives an **augmented fourth** (F–B), which **expands** to a sixth by the same tendency tones moving outward.

### The Minor Seventh (G–F): The Pull Downward

The seventh (F) has a strong melodic tendency to descend by step to E (the third of I). Combined with B→C, the complete resolution is:
- G (root) → can move to double the tonic
- B (third/leading-tone) → up to C
- D (fifth) → has no strong tendency; moves down to C or up to E to avoid doubling the modal third
- F (seventh) → down to E

---

## Regular Resolution: V₇ → I

```
V7:  G  B  D  F
      ↓  ↑  ↓  ↓
I:   C  C  C  E   (incomplete — three roots, one third, no fifth)
```

**Standard result: I without fifth, with three roots and one third.** This is always preferred over doubling the modal third. The tonal balance of three roots and one third is harmonically stronger than two roots and two thirds.

> *"The presence of the fourth degree, subdominant, supplies the only important tonal factor that was missing in V–I; hence this is the perfect progression for the definition of tonality."*

### Incomplete V₇ (omitting the fifth)

The **fifth (D)** may be omitted from V₇, with the root (G) doubled instead. This gives a convenient common tone with I, allows a complete tonic chord in resolution, and is frequently used in root position.

### V₇ → I6 (alternate resolution)

When V₇ resolves to I in **first inversion**, the bass takes over the resolution of the seventh — the seventh may move upward rather than down. This causes the seventh to resolve "incorrectly" in the melodic sense but the contrapuntal logic of the first inversion bass compensates. Less common but valid.

## The Four Positions of V₇

| Symbol | Bass note | Name | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| **V₇** | Root (G) | "Dominant seventh" | Standard; completes by dropping seventh |
| **V⁶₅** | 3rd (B = leading-tone) | "Dominant six-five" | Most effective with seventh in soprano; bass moves to tonic |
| **V⁴₃** | 5th (D) | "Four-three" | Passing chord; bass between I and I6 |
| **V₂** | 7th (F = subdominant) | "Two" or "four-two" | Strongest; bass has strong downward pull to E → resolves to **I6** |

### V⁶₅ — First Inversion (leading-tone in bass)

The leading-tone in the bass gives the bass **strong upward melodic significance** (same as V6 from Ch. 9). The next chord will almost certainly be I (or I6). Most effective when the original seventh (F) is in the soprano voice.

Tendency tones resolve identically to root position — the inversion doesn't change the harmonic behavior, only the bass line.

### V⁴₃ — Second Inversion (fifth in bass)

Used chiefly as a **passing chord** — bass passes between tonic and third degree. The fifth (D) is the lower note of the dissonant perfect fourth in this position, so it is free to move either up or down. When the bass moves up to the third degree, the seventh resolves upward — the same result as V⁶₅ → I6.

Rhythmically **weak** — it is a transitional chord, not a structural one.

### V₂ — Third Inversion (seventh in bass)

The **strongest and most directional** of the inversions. The seventh (F) is in the bass and is dissonant with two upper voices simultaneously:
- Augmented fourth (F–B)
- Major second (F–G, or its inversion the minor seventh)

The bass has an **unusually strong tendency to move down by one step** — F → E, which means the resolution is always to **I6** (tonic in first inversion, E in bass).

A favorite soprano part for this progression: the supertonic (D) moves up a fourth to the dominant (G), creating an open, rising quality against the descending bass.

## Preparation of the Dissonance

Four degrees of preparation, from most to least prepared:

| Method | Description | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **(a) Suspension** | The seventh is tied over from the previous chord | Most prepared; the seventh pre-exists |
| **(b) Repeated tone** | The seventh is repeated in the same voice | Prepared, not tied |
| **(c) By step** | The seventh enters by step from the preceding note | Less prepared |
| **(d) By skip** | The seventh enters by skip — unprepared | Least prepared; more abrupt |

Common-practice composers show considerable variety and inconsistency in their treatment of preparation. Bach uses both prepared and unprepared dissonances freely, choosing based on the musical context rather than a rule. The student is advised to practice all four approaches.

---

## The Melodic Tritone

Any melodic fragment spanning an augmented fourth (tritone = subdominant to leading-tone, F to B in C major) is **almost invariably resolved**:

- **Ascending tritone** (F→B): the leading-tone B continues up to C (tonic). The augmented fourth expands out to a sixth.
- **Descending tritone** (B→F): B continues up to C (leading-tone resolution upward), F continues down to E (subdominant falls to third of tonic). The line resolves inward.

**Unresolved melodic tritones are avoided.** If the F–B tritone appears melodically and neither tendency tone resolves, the result sounds unfinished and directionally unclear.

## FormaComposition Implications

**V₇ is the single most important chord in FormaComposition's output after the triad.** Every cadence that includes a dominant should include the seventh — it adds the subdominant (F in C major) that makes the key-defining function of V complete. V without the seventh implies the key; V7 confirms it.

**The fifth of V₇ is regularly omitted — this is correct, not an error.** A V7 chord with root, third, and seventh (no fifth) resolving to a complete I chord is the standard arrangement. FormaComposition's voicing logic should know this: omitting D from G–B–D–F and doubling G gives G–B–G–F, which resolves cleanly to C–C–C–E (complete I).

**V₂ always resolves to I6, never I root position.** If the engine assigns V2 (F in bass) as the last chord of a section that ends on I root position, that is incorrect. The bass F must fall to E — hence I6. This is a validator check: V₂ → I must become V₂ → I6.

**The four inversions give the bass voice four different melodic approaches to the dominant.** V₇ = bass holds or steps; V⁶₅ = bass is the leading-tone, ascends; V⁴₃ = bass is passing, neutral; V₂ = bass is the seventh, descends by semitone. Choosing between them is a bass line decision, not a harmonic one. The harmony is identical in all four cases.

**The melodic tritone must resolve.** If the melody voice in FormaComposition spans an augmented fourth (e.g., from F4 to B4), the following note must resolve the leading-tone tendency — either B continues to C, or the fragment is structured to avoid the unresolved tritone. This is a melodic constraint, not just harmonic.

**Preparation of the seventh is a stylistic parameter.** Early sections of a piece (establishing the style) should probably use prepared or step-approached V7s. As the piece develops, unprepared (skip) approach to the seventh increases tension and urgency. This maps to the `arc` field — rising arc → more unprepared dissonances; settling arc → more prepared.
