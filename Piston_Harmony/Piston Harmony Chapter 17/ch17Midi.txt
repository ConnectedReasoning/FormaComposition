ex1 — the critical comparison: dim7 (B D F Ab) resolving to I, then incomplete major ninth (B D F A natural) resolving to I, then both back to back before the final tonic. One semitone difference, but the harmonic character shifts from the hollow, equalised sound of the dim7 to the more open, slightly wistful quality of the half-diminished.
ex2 — the characteristic resolution technique: first V°9 resolving directly (less typical), then the "ninth resolves first" version where A steps down to G before the chord proper resolves. The melodic pre-resolution of the ninth is the signature gesture of this chord.
ex3 — all four positions: B bass → I, D bass → I6, F bass → I, A bass → V7 (the rarely used ninth-in-bass version, where A acts as a bass suspension resolving to G). Each sounds notably different — unlike the dim7 inversions, these positions have distinct characters.
ex4 — the tension gradient: same setup, then V7, then V°9 (half-dim), then dim7. Each steps up in harmonic intensity. Useful for making compositional decisions about which level of dominant tension a given moment needs.
ex5 — V°9ofV as secondary dominant: plain half cadence, then V°9ofV sharpening the dominant arrival, then in a full cadential context. The F# in the chord gives it a slightly different color than the plain VofV triad.
ex6 — sequences: the half-dim chord repeated three times in a descending sequence before the authentic cadence. Harmonic sequences using this chord are characteristic of late Baroque and Romantic writing.
ex7 — complete phrase incorporating both the main V°9 and secondary V°9ofV in a musical context.
ex8 — the Romantic color file: slow, held half-dim chords across all three positions, resolving very softly to I. Assigned to a slow instrument (Mellotron strings, Solina) this is the sound of the Tristan chord aesthetic — atmospheric, suspended, melancholic. Worth sitting with through Logic.
