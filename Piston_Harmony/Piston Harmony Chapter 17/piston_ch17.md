# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 17
## The Incomplete Major Ninth

> *A tendency is noticeable throughout the period of harmonic common practice to treat the upper tone of the major ninth interval, with or without root, as a melodic tone, resolving it to the tone below before the resolution of the chord takes place.*

---

## Two Incomplete Dominant Ninths

The dominant ninth chord (V9 = G B D F A in C major) appears in two forms without its root:

| Form | Notes (C major) | Name |
|---|---|---|
| **V°9b** (minor ninth) | B D F **Ab** | Diminished seventh chord (Ch. 16) |
| **V°9** (major ninth) | B D F **A** | Incomplete dominant major ninth |

These are the two "dominant ninths without root." Both resolve to the tonic triad. But they differ significantly:

- In the **diminished seventh**, all thirds are minor thirds — all inversions sound harmonically alike
- In the **incomplete major ninth** (V°9), the interval B–A is a **major seventh** (= major third between two upper factors), giving each inversion a **markedly different character**

The V°9 chord in C major: **B D F A** — called a "half-diminished seventh chord" in modern terminology. Intervals: minor third (B–D), diminished fifth (B–F), minor seventh (B–A).

---

## The Positions

The V°9 chord has four positions, each with a distinct sound unlike the enharmonically equivalent inversions of the dim7:

| Position | Symbol | Bass | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leading-tone in bass | **V°9** | B | Standard; most typical with A (major 9th) in soprano |
| D in bass | V°⁶₅ | D | Useful for melodic bass |
| F in bass | V°⁴₃ | F | Six-four-three; resolves to I or I6 |
| Ninth in bass | V°₂ | A | **Rarely used** — ninth in bass acts as suspension resolving to dominant root |

The **last inversion** (ninth = A in bass) appears almost exclusively as a dominant seventh chord where the sixth degree acts as a **suspension in the bass**, resolving down to G (the dominant root). The V°₂ is therefore really an embellished V7 rather than an independent chord.

---

## Resolution: The Ninth's Melodic Tendency

The major ninth (A above the chord) has a characteristic tendency: it is treated throughout the common-practice period as a **melodic tone** — it resolves down to the tone below (G, the dominant) **before** the chord itself resolves to the tonic. The ninth steps down first, then the chord resolves.

When the ninth does remain through the resolution:
- The typical disposition has A (major 9th) **in the soprano** with the leading-tone B present in the chord
- This is where the characteristic sonority of V°9 is heard at its best

### Regular Resolution: V°9 → I

The two diminished fifths (B–F and D–A... wait, B–F and D–A, where D–A is a minor seventh inversely... B–D–F–A) — the diminished fifth B–F contracts to a third. 

**Parallel fifth danger:** In the standard resolution, the second degree (D) moving to the third degree (E) while the bass moves to C can produce parallel fifths between bass and the voice moving from D to E. This is avoided by:
- Moving D **up** to E (melodic approach to third)
- Or moving D **down by skip** to the dominant (G)

In neither case will the root be doubled in the tonic chord.

If the interval of a fifth is inverted to a fourth, all three upper voices may descend in parallel motion.

---

## Contrast with Diminished Seventh

Unlike the dim7, the incomplete major ninth is **not equally appropriate to both modes**:

- The major sixth degree (A natural in C major) is a dissonance that tends to descend
- In the **minor mode**, this descent is problematic: it would create a cross-relation with the leading-tone (tritone between A natural and Eb/Ab)
- If the sixth degree were purely melodic in minor, it could ascend as the ascending melodic minor scale, but would lose its harmonic significance as a ninth

Therefore: **V°9 (major ninth form) is primarily a major-mode chord**.

---

## Available in These Degrees

The degrees that may be preceded by a dominant with a **major ninth**:
- In **major mode**: I, IV, V
- In **minor mode**: III, V, VI

For III in C major: the dominant with major ninth would be the same as the seventh chord on the second degree (II7 in C). This creates an interesting ambiguity.

---

## Secondary Dominants

The incomplete dominant major ninth is **far less useful as a secondary dominant** than the diminished seventh chord, because it cannot serve as the dominant of a minor tonic. The most common in this form:

**V°9ofV** (most common) — leads to the dominant, typically in a half cadence or as preparation for the authentic cadence.

The degrees which may have the major ninth as secondary dominant are limited to those with major-mode resolution, meaning:
- V°9ofIV (= same as VII7 in C major / the chord built on the leading-tone)
- V°9ofV (D F# A C in C major — resolves to G major context)
- V°9ofVI

---

## Modulation

The V°9 chord offers **limited modulation opportunities** compared to the dim7 — no enharmonic changes are possible. The V°9 of C major has these interpretations:
- **VofIV** in G major (= same as VII7 in G)
- **VofV** in F major
- **VofVI** in E major
- **II7** in A major

Only four interpretations versus the dim7's twelve (or forty-eight). The chord before or after the V°9 usually provides a better pivot chord for modulation.

---

## Irregular Resolutions

The V°9 (incomplete major ninth) is **not capable of greatly varied irregular resolution**:

- **→ II**: Weak — all tones of II are common to both chords; the seventh doesn't resolve
- **→ III**: The chord loses its identity as a dominant ninth and becomes the seventh chord on the leading-tone (VII7). Most often seen in harmonic sequences.
- **→ IV**: Possible if subdominant has tonic in bass — this is really regular resolution in disguise (the tonic in the bass of IV makes it function as I6, and the resolution becomes V°9 → I6)
- **→ VI**: Not more satisfactory; if tonic is in the bass of VI, it represents I and is regular; if VI is in second inversion, the V°9 is really VII7; if VI is in root position, both chords sound like II–I in minor mode

## The Open Score Digression

Piston uses this chapter to introduce a note on **open score** (four separate staves for SATB), advocating that students work in open score to make each voice's melodic and rhythmic line more readily apparent. The condensed two-stave system used in exercises obscures the linear structure; open score reveals it.

## FormaComposition Implications

**The V°9 (half-diminished leading-tone 7th) is a distinct harmonic color from the dim7.** In MIDI terms, B D F A versus B D F Ab differs by one semitone, but the harmonic character is significantly lighter and less dark. The half-diminished chord has a characteristically open, slightly ambiguous quality often associated with the Romantic era — it appears prominently in Tristan und Isolde (the "Tristan chord" is a half-diminished chord).

**The ninth's melodic resolution before the chord resolves is a standard technique.** If the engine assigns V°9 (incomplete major ninth), the soprano or top voice should typically resolve the A down to G before the rest of the chord moves to I. This "ornamental resolution" — the ninth stepping down first — is one of the most characteristic melodic gestures associated with this chord.

**V°9ofV is the most practically useful secondary dominant in this form.** In C major: D F# A C (= half-diminished chord on D, pointing to G). This creates a distinctive half-cadence color distinct from the regular VofV (D F# A) or V7ofV (D F# A C with different interpretation).

**The chord is primarily major-mode.** Unlike the dim7 which works freely in both major and minor, the V°9 should be used carefully in minor contexts. In FormaComposition, mode-checking before deploying this chord as a secondary dominant is warranted.
