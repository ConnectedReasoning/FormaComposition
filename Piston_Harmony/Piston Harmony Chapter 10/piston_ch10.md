# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 10
## Nonharmonic Tones

> *The contrapuntal material of music comprises all the melodic tones and rhythms which are interwoven to make the texture. Some of these tones appear as factors of the chords and some do not. Those that do not are customarily called nonharmonic tones.*

---

## What They Are — and What They Really Are

"Nonharmonic tone" is technically a contradiction — literally, there is no such thing, since any tone sounding with others creates harmony. The term is a useful analytical shorthand. During the common-practice period, composers were so chord-minded that they created melodies from chord tones with nonchord tones added for embellishment. So we can divide tones into chord tones (structural) and non-chord tones (ornamental).

**The true nature of nonharmonic tones is melodic.** Their character is not derived from their foreignness to the harmony; it comes from the melodic line itself — its rhythm, direction, and contour. They can be identified by studying the melody alone.

> *"The student is advised to study the works of Sebastian Bach as models of balance in the use of all nonharmonic tones."*

Too many nonharmonic tones obscure the harmonic framework and weaken the music. Use them to clarify and animate, not to obscure.

---

## The Seven Types

### 1. Passing Tone (PT)
**Fills in a skip with stepwise motion**, either diatonic or chromatic.

- Approached by step from the preceding note; continues by step to the next
- Rhythmically **WEAK** (unaccented) — may appear on any beat but without metric stress
- The interval filled need not be between two tones of the same chord
- "Accented passing tone" — a passing tone on a metrically stressed position — is really an appoggiatura
- In the minor mode: when used as passing tones, the 6th and 7th degrees are drawn from the **melodic minor scale** (ascending and descending forms accordingly)
- Passing tones in multiple voices simultaneously create complexity; Bach is the model of using this without obscuring the harmonic skeleton

### 2. Auxiliary Tone (AUX)
Also called: neighboring note, embellishment, *broderie*.

**Ornaments a stationary tone** — leaves by a half or whole step above or below, then returns to the same tone.

- Approached and quitted by step, returning to the main tone
- Rhythmically **WEAK**
- Can be **chromatically altered**: the lower auxiliary especially benefits from raising by a half-step, giving it the character of a temporary leading-tone to the main tone
- **Upper + lower auxiliary** together form a **turn** (five-note figure: main → above → main → below → main)
- **Double auxiliary** = turn with the middle note omitted (upper → main tone → lower, or reverse)
- Multiple auxiliaries in all voices simultaneously = an **auxiliary chord** — a well-known harmonic effect in slow, sustained textures

### 3. Appoggiatura (APP)
From Italian *appoggiare* = to lean.

**The only rhythmically strong nonharmonic tone.** All others are weak; the appoggiatura is the exception.

- Approached by any interval (not necessarily step); resolves **by half or whole step** to the main tone
- Rhythm is invariably **strong-to-weak**: the appoggiatura falls on the stronger beat, the resolution on the weaker
- Creates the impression of leaning heavily on the tone toward which it resolves
- **Avoid doubling the note of resolution** in the other voices, especially when the appoggiatura is not in the top voice
- Can be **chromatically raised** (approaching resolution by leading-tone semitone) or **chromatically lowered** (most pungent form — the lowered seventh degree standing for the sixth against a leading-tone in dominant harmony, creating a diminished octave)
- Several appoggiaturae simultaneously = an **appoggiatura chord**

### 4. Suspension (SUSP)
**A tone whose natural progression has been rhythmically delayed.**

- The note is **tied over** from the previous chord into the new chord — the tie is characteristic; it is the mechanism of the delay
- Creates dissonance against the new chord; then resolves (usually downward by step)
- Rhythm of the suspending voice: **strong-to-weak, long-to-short**
- Rhythm of the harmony: **weak-to-strong** (the new chord arrives on the strong beat)
- These two rhythms move in opposite directions — this conflict is the characteristic suspension tension
- **Upward resolution**: not infrequent when the suspended note is a leading-tone or chromatically raised tone
- Multiple simultaneous suspensions = a **suspended chord** — creates the familiar effect of two harmonies sounding at once, common in slow cadences
- **Ornamental resolutions**: before the true note of resolution arrives, the suspension may pass through an auxiliary, échappée, cambiata, or anticipation

### 5. Échappée (ECH)
From French *échapper* = to escape.

**A note that escapes from the direction of melodic movement, then returns by skip.**

- Most commonly: approached by step, then skips in the **opposite direction** to the destination
- Application: usually interpolated between a tendency tone and its resolution — the voice steps toward the destination, then "escapes" in the opposite direction before arriving
- Effect: a small melodic swerve, giving the line a hint of reluctance before arriving

### 6. Cambiata (CAMB)
From Italian *cambiare* = to change.

**A note that has gone too far past its destination and must return by step.**

- Approached by step; overshoots the destination by one step; then **returns by step** to the destination
- The reverse pattern from échappée: instead of escaping, it oversteps and corrects
- Both échappée and cambiata are most naturally understood as ornamental elaborations of a basic stepwise melodic progression

### 7. Anticipation (ANT)
**Sounds the next chord tone before the chord arrives** — an advance sounding.

- Rhythmically like an **upbeat** to the anticipated tone
- Usually **not tied** to the principal tone (if tied, it becomes a suspension in reverse)
- Ordinarily **much shorter** in time value than the principal tone
- Creates a sense of forward lean — the melody arrives early, the harmony catches up

## Rhythmic Summary

| Type | Rhythmic Weight | Approach | Departure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Passing tone | **Weak** | Step | Step (same direction) |
| Auxiliary | **Weak** | Step | Step (return to origin) |
| Appoggiatura | **STRONG** | Any interval | Step (half or whole) |
| Suspension | **Strong beat, held** | Tied over | Step down (usually) |
| Échappée | **Weak** | Step | Skip (opposite direction) |
| Cambiata | **Weak** | Step | Step (return after overshoot) |
| Anticipation | **Weak (upbeat)** | Any | — (becomes principal tone) |

## The Constructive Process

Piston's four-step composition method at the end of the chapter — the complete pipeline from roots to ornamented texture:

1. **Adopt a tonality and root sequence** — e.g., B-flat major: I–IV–II–V–I–V, III–IV–I–II–III–V–I
2. **Construct a bass** — apply harmonic rhythm, use root position and first inversions to give the bass melodic motion
3. **Add three upper voices** — simple, block-chord realization
4. **Apply nonharmonic tones** to all four voices — "without limit as to possible variants, although it is useless to strive for complexity"

> *"This step is practically without limit as to possible variants."*

The underlying harmonic structure stays constant through all four steps. The nonharmonic tones are surface decoration. The composer controls the density of decoration — from bare block chords to elaborate counterpoint — without changing a single root.

## FormaComposition Implications

**Nonharmonic tones are the difference between a chord chart and a melody.** The FormaComposition melody voice currently selects from chord tones and scale degrees. Adding passing tones (fill skips with steps), auxiliary tones (ornament held notes), and appoggiaturas (stress-approach to chord tones) would immediately make the melodic output more expressive.

**The appoggiatura is the only strong nonharmonic tone — and it's the most expressive one.** For FormaComposition's melody voice, generating an occasional appoggiatura (a note slightly foreign to the chord, landing on the beat, resolving by step) is the single highest-value addition to melodic expressiveness. It's the "lean" that makes a melody feel like it has emotion.

**The suspension is a cross-voice timing event.** It requires the melody to hold a note across a chord change, creating dissonance against the new chord before resolving. FormaComposition currently doesn't model this — the melody realigns with each new chord. A "suspension flag" on a section or beat would tell the melody voice to hold its previous note through the chord change, then resolve by step. This is one of the most powerful effects in tonal music for creating tension at cadences.

**The Piston four-step process is the FormaComposition generation order.** Step 1 = `progression` + `chord_bars`. Step 2 = bass voice with `harmony_pattern`. Step 3 = harmony + counterpoint voices. Step 4 = melody voice with nonharmonic tone logic. The chain is explicit and intentional — each layer is built on the previous, and the nonharmonic layer is always last and always optional.

**"Too many nonharmonic tones obscure the harmonic framework."** This is the argument against complex prosody on every beat of every bar. The `density` field in FormaComposition controls how much melodic activity there is — low density = mostly chord tones (clear structure), high density = more passing and auxiliary tones (active but potentially busy). Getting the density balance right is a compositional judgment, not a technical one.

**The auxiliary chord is a named effect.** When multiple voices simultaneously sound auxiliary tones above a held chord, the result is an auxiliary chord — a momentary "cloud" of dissonance that resolves back to the original. This is exactly the kind of effect Valhalla Shimmer creates by smearing the harmony — an analog to the acoustic auxiliary chord. Knowing it has a name gives it compositional legitimacy.
