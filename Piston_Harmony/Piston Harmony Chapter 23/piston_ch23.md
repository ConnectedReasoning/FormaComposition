# Piston — Harmony: Chapter 23
## The Neapolitan Sixth

> *It is a chord of strongly subdominant character, often used in cadences to introduce the dominant. By far the commonest progression from the Neapolitan sixth is to the dominant chord in some form.*

---

## What the Neapolitan Sixth Is

The **Neapolitan sixth** (symbol: **N₆** or **II⁶ᵇ**) is the major triad built on the **lowered second degree** of the scale, normally found in **first inversion** (hence "sixth").

In C major:
```
Root position: Db – F – Ab  (= Db major triad)
First inversion (typical): F – Ab – Db  (F in bass)
```

The name is historical and not particularly accurate — the chord appears throughout European music and is not specifically Neapolitan. The label is nonetheless universal and convenient.

**Structure:** A major triad — not dissonant. Its chromatic quality comes from the **lowered second degree** (Db in C major), which gives it a downward tendency. Piston notes that when this lowered tone proceeds upward (as it sometimes does), there is "a feeling of irregularity of movement."

**Character:** Strongly **subdominant** — the F in the bass of the first inversion is the subdominant of C, and the whole chord partakes of subdominant function.

---

## Doubling

In four-part writing, the **bass note** (= the fourth degree in first inversion) is the best tone to double, as it is a tonal degree. The lowered second degree is normally **not doubled** in first inversion — it has strong melodic tendency and doubling it creates voice-leading problems. However, this rule is followed less strictly for the Neapolitan sixth than for other chromatic altered chords.

---

## The Cross-Relation Problem

The progression N₆ → V contains the **cross-relation** between the lowered second degree (Db) and the natural second degree (D) that appears in the V chord. This cross-relation is widely found in the literature — composers do not consistently avoid it. Several strategies exist:

1. **Let the cross-relation occur** — it is idiomatic to this progression
2. **Place both tones in one voice** — the Db descends to C (or elsewhere) while D appears in another voice, keeping the chromatic motion in a single line
3. **Use the cadential I64** as an intermediate step — inserting the tonic six-four between N₆ and V causes the cross-relation to be unnoticeable (the intervening tonic harmony absorbs it)
4. **Use V9 or V°9** (dim7) — if the dominant chord contains no fifth, no cross-relation arises

When the dominant contains a seventh, its fifth will very likely be omitted to avoid cross-relation.

---

## Paths to the Dominant

The N₆ most commonly progresses to V, but several paths exist:

### Direct: N₆ → V
Three voice-leading arrangements possible (see Ex. 504). The bass moves from the subdominant up a perfect fourth to the dominant.

### Via I64: N₆ → I64 → V
The cadential six-four acts as a double appoggiatura over the dominant bass, allowing smooth stepwise motion in the three upper voices while the bass proceeds V-ward. The cross-relation between Db and D is unnoticeable because of the intervening tonic harmony.

### Via V9 or dim7: N₆ → V9 → V or N₆ → V°9 → V
The third inversion of V7 (V2) is also possible when the bass remains stationary while the harmony changes.

### Via other chords: N₆ → I₆ or N₆ → VofV
The N₆ is occasionally used as subdominant harmony → I or VofIV, not exclusively pointing to V.

### The Plagal Function: N₆ → I or N₆ → VofIV
The Neapolitan sixth's subdominant character allows it to precede the tonic directly or to function as subdominant harmony in a plagal cadence.

---

## The Neapolitan in Root Position

In the 19th century, the chord was increasingly used **in root position** (Db in the bass) rather than the traditional first inversion. This gave it more harmonic independence and stability, treating the lowered second degree as a true harmonic root rather than a melodic tendency tone. The doubled root and the augmented fourth relationship to the dominant (Db to G) emphasize the chord's remoteness from the main tonal center.

Beethoven's Sonata Op. 111 and Schubert's Piano Sonata in C minor are famous examples of the root-position Neapolitan used with full harmonic weight.

---

## Uses Beyond the Cadence

The Neapolitan sixth is not limited to cadential formulae:
- It may appear **anywhere in the phrase**
- It may even **begin the piece** (its subdominant character can precede the initial tonic)
- As a **coloristic chord** in predominantly major-mode passages — its Db (in C major) creates a striking dark shadow in a major key context

When used over a tonic pedal, the N₆ becomes a kind of double appoggiatura effect (the F and Ab are appoggiaturas to E and G of the tonic triad).

---

## Mode

The Neapolitan sixth is used in both major and minor modes, but is more characteristic of **minor**. In minor the lowered second degree (Db in C minor) belongs naturally to the Phrygian mode; in major it is a more distinctly chromatic intrusion. The chord is at home in both but sounds darker and more characteristic in minor contexts.

---

## False Modulation

The Neapolitan sixth chord "is often felt to be of sufficient tonal strength to cause a momentary shift to its root as a tonal center." This produces a **false modulation** — the tonality is temporarily suspended or shifted to the Neapolitan's root (Db in C major), then immediately returns to the main key.

The false modulation differs from a passing modulation in that it returns to the original key rather than proceeding to a third key.

In analysis: when a passage of harmonies strongly suggests the key of the Neapolitan's root, consider whether the whole passage is better analyzed as a false modulation or as relationships within the main tonality. Neither analysis is wrong; the harmonic effect is what matters.

---

## The Extension of the Secondary Dominant Principle

Piston introduces here an important analytical insight. The Neapolitan in root position can be called **IIofII** — the major supertonic of the supertonic. In Neapolitan sixth contexts, chords related to the Neapolitan's root as secondary dominants (VofN₆, IVofN₆ etc.) are sometimes present. The secondary dominant principle can be extended this way.

More generally: the **strength of the tonal center** is demonstrated by the ability to include all chords into their relationship to the main tonality, rather than declaring every foreign chord a modulation. This is the principle that will "eventually include all chords into any given tonality" as 20th-century harmonic thinking expands.

---

## Modulation

As a simple major triad, the Neapolitan sixth is capable of many interpretations in other keys — common ground between distantly related keys. In the following key relationships, N₆ of C = Db major = the same chord appears as:
- **II of Cb major** (very distant)
- **V of Gb major** 
- **I of Db major** (the root position Neapolitan becomes the tonic)
- **IV of Ab major**

This interpretive flexibility makes the Neapolitan sixth a useful pivot chord for modulations between keys a half-step, tritone, or major/minor third apart — typical Romantic-era distant modulations.

---

## FormaComposition Implications

**The Neapolitan sixth is the single most distinctive non-diatonic chord in tonal music.** The Db major triad in C major or C minor — with that flat-second root — is immediately recognizable as a "Neapolitan" sound. It carries strong emotional associations: darkness, pathos, a kind of harmonic sigh before the dominant.

**II⁶ᵇ → I64 → V7 → I is the fullest cadential formula with the Neapolitan.** The insertion of I64 between the Neapolitan and V resolves the cross-relation and creates one of the most satisfying cadential approach sequences in tonal music. FormaComposition should know this as a named four-chord pattern.

**The Neapolitan appears freely in any part of the phrase.** It is not exclusively cadential. A phrase that begins on a Neapolitan sixth, then proceeds to I, IV, V, I has a dramatically different color from one beginning on I directly. For ambient music, the Neapolitan introduces an immediate harmonic shadow.

**Minor mode is the natural home.** When FormaComposition is in a minor key, using II⁶ᵇ at a cadence is a natural, stylistically appropriate choice. In a major key, the same chord is a more distinctly chromatic interruption.

**The false modulation effect creates section-to-section tension.** If a section lingers on N₆ long enough to suggest the Db key briefly, before resolving back home, a "false modulation" has occurred — a kind of harmonic detour that strengthens the eventual return. This can be built into a FormaComposition section by simply holding the Neapolitan chord for longer.

**Root position Neapolitan = full harmonic statement.** The first-inversion N₆ is traditional and voice-leading-smooth; the root-position N (Db in bass) is bold, stark, and more modern-feeling. The choice of inversion signals compositional intent.
