ex1 — the core move of the whole chapter: one melody note (E) heard three times, each time under a different chord that contains it — as root of III, third of I, fifth of VI. Same pitch, three completely different harmonic contexts. This is the root/third/fifth framework made audible.
ex2 — melodic skip: C→G skip under one sustained I chord (natural, both are chord tones), then the same skip with a chord change where the second note lands on a strong beat (justified by harmonic rhythm). Hear why skips usually stay on one chord.
ex3 — sustained E held for four whole bars while the harmony moves I→III→VI→I underneath it. The melody is motionless; the harmony provides all the motion. A technique worth using deliberately.
ex4 — the same four ascending notes (C D E F) harmonized three ways: mechanical one-chord-per-note (flat, obvious), improved version (hold I across passing tones), and best version (VI as the "unexpected" moment before IV). Three versions, same melody.
ex5 — formula recognition: the same four-note ascending figure (G A B C) as upper voice of three different harmonic formulae. The melody is identical; the harmonic world underneath it changes completely.
ex6 — bad bass vs good bass. First version: bass just plays roots, skips everywhere, no melodic identity. Second version: bass moves stepwise C-D-E-F-E-D-C in contrary motion to the melody. The difference is immediate — the second version sounds like music, the first like a chord chart.
ex7 — a complete 8-bar harmonization: antecedent phrase ending on V (half cadence) answered by consequent ending on I (authentic). The VI in bar 3 of the antecedent is the "unexpected" moment Piston requires. Load this into Logic's piano roll to see the voice leading across all four parts.
