# "Return" — A Test of Statefulness in Practice

## Composition Overview

**Key:** D Mixolydian (D E F# G A B C)  
**Duration:** ~5 minutes  
**Tempo:** 62 BPM  
**Concept:** Returning to a place that's home but changed.

---

## Why D Mixolydian?

- **Major tonality** (D-F#-A) = warmth, openness
- **Flattened 7** (C natural) = bittersweet, floating quality
- **The b7 is never resolved** — it just lives there. Perfect metaphor for returning home and finding it's not quite the same.

---

## Motif: "Return" Prosody

```
R  E  T  U  R  N
-  .  .  -  .  .
re  turn
(unstressed, stressed)
```

**Intervals:** [-2, 4, 1, -3]
- Down 2 semitones (approach)
- Up 4 semitones (the leap to the stressed syllable)
- Up 1 semitone (continuation)
- Down 3 semitones (resolution back)

**Rhythm:** [0.5, 1.0, 0.5, 1.0]
- Short, long emphasis (mirrors the stress pattern)

**Transforms:**
- Augmentation: stretches the arrival (makes it linger)
- Retrograde: collapses the arrival (reverses the journey)
- Inversion: questions the arrival (turns it upside down)

This motif should *sound* like arriving at a door. Every phrase is a gesture of arrival.

## Section-by-Section Breakdown

### 1. Threshold (12 bars, fade_in)

**Progression:** I - V - vi (D - A - Bm)  
**Density:** Sparse  
**Bass:** Pedal (D held throughout)

**Statefulness moment:** None yet. This is the question — should I go back?

**What to listen for:**
- The melody barely moves. Just fragments of the motif, not complete.
- Long silences. Space is part of the music.
- Bass never moves from D. It's grounding, not moving forward.
- The progression I-V-vi is open, questioning. Not resolved.

**Why it works:** Absence of statefulness creates hesitation. The melody doesn't "know" what's coming, so it's careful.

---

### 2. Passage (16 bars, swell)

**Progression:** V - bVI - IV - V (A - Bbmaj7 - G - A)  
**Density:** Medium  
**Bass:** Root-fifth (moving now)  
**Counterpoint:** Yes (free, below, passing dissonance)

**Statefulness moment:** THE KEY MOMENT

**The deceptive cadence:** V→bVI
- Traditional expectation: V wants to resolve to I (home)
- Actual: V goes to bVI (bittersweet, not-quite-home)
- This is the composition's emotional center: returning and finding the place *changed*

**With statefulness:**
- Melody "knows" next chord is bVI
- At the end of the V phrase, melody should *lean toward* bVI tones
- It shouldn't feel surprised by the arrival of bVI; it should anticipate it
- This creates *voice-leading gravity* toward the compositional intention

**Without statefulness (original behavior):**
- Melody generates over V in isolation
- It might land on A (V root) or E (V fifth) expecting resolution to I
- When bVI arrives, it feels unexpected, wrong, or "generated"

**Why this matters:** This is where statefulness *proves itself*. The same harmony, but with context-aware melody, feels intentional instead of accidental.

---

### 3. Recognition (20 bars, breath)

**Progression:** I - IV - V - I - bVII - IV (D - G - A - D - C - G)  
**Density:** Full  
**Melody:** Develop (motif transforms)  
**Bass:** Walking  
**Arc:** Breath (natural swell and recede)

**Statefulness moment:** The bVII reveal

**The bVII chord (C major):**
- This is the D Mixolydian color revealed
- It's not chromatic or borrowed; it's part of the mode
- But it's *weird* at this moment — home (D), then this ghost note (C)
- Melody should recognize: "This C is intentional, part of the place"

**What happens:**
- Walking bass drives the motion (feels like walking through rooms)
- Melody develops the motif (augmentation stretches it, inversion questions it)
- Counterpoint breathes underneath (adds texture without competing)
- The bVII lands soft, accepted — not as a surprise, but as a realization

**Why statefulness helps:**
- Melody knows the progression in advance
- When bVII approaches, melody can shape itself to *land on* the C
- This makes C feel like home (or at least, like it belongs here)
- Without statefulness, melody might avoid C (treating it as chromatic)

---

### 4. Settling (16 bars, fade_out)

**Progression:** I - bVII - IV - I (D - C - G - D)  
**Density:** Sparse  
**Melody:** Sparse  
**Bass:** Root-only (single notes, minimal motion)

**Statefulness moment:** The cycle completes, but nothing resolves

**What happens:**
- Melody echoes the motif, fragmented (maybe retrograded or inverted)
- Bass is just roots: D, C, G, D (no movement, just anchors)
- The piece doesn't resolve the Mixolydian ambiguity; it just accepts it
- Fade is gradual — the piece becomes silence, not ending

**Why statefulness helps:**
- Sparse melody needs to avoid collisions with sparse bass
- Statefulness lets melody "know" what bass note is coming
- Each melody gesture can arc into or away from the bass note
- This prevents awkward unison or tritone clashes

**The final thought:** The piece cycles I - bVII - IV - I. This is a loop, not a resolution. Coming home means *living with* the strangeness, not fixing it.

---

## Where Statefulness Matters Most

1. **Passage section (V→bVI deceptive cadence)**
   - Melody should anticipate bVI, lean toward it
   - Makes the "false resolution" feel compositional, not generated
   - *This is the test case*

2. **Recognition section (bVII reveal)**
   - Melody should recognize C natural as intentional
   - Not treating it as chromatic mistake
   - Landing on C should feel like arrival, not accident

3. **Settling section (sparse texture)**
   - Melody + bass are both sparse, lots of space
   - Statefulness prevents harmonic collisions
   - Each note placement is deliberate

---

## How to Evaluate "Return"

Generate it. Listen 3+ times.

**With statefulness, you should hear:**
1. **Passage:** Deceptive cadence feels *intended*, not surprising
2. **Recognition:** bVII (C major) feels like home, not a mistake
3. **Settling:** Each sparse note feels placed, not random

**Without statefulness (revert to original), you might hear:**
1. **Passage:** Melody seems caught off-guard by bVI
2. **Recognition:** C natural feels chromatic, not modal
3. **Settling:** Sparse notes feel scattered, not deliberate

**The question:** Does statefulness make the piece feel more *composed* and less *generated*?

If yes → the pattern works. Move to bass statefulness next.  
If no → listen more carefully. Maybe statefulness isn't the missing piece.

## Future: Stateful Prosody Evolution

This piece is a foundation for the next idea: **prosody that evolves with the composition.**

**Future possibility:**
- **Threshold:** "Return" motif at *minimum intensity* (sparse stress)
- **Passage:** "Return" motif at *full intensity* (compressed, emphasized stress)
- **Recognition:** "Return" motif at *resolution intensity* (stress dispersed, acceptance)
- **Settling:** "Return" motif at *echoing intensity* (retrograded, fading)

Same word. Same root. But **the stress pattern evolves to match emotional arc.**

## Notes for Future Iteration

- The false resolution V→bVI is the *entire piece's idea*. Make sure it hits.
- bVII should feel like a color, not a mistake. The Mixolydian nature is the whole point.
- Sparse sections need space. Don't fill silence. Silence is the composition.
- The motif transforms (augmentation, retrograde, inversion) should preserve the arrival gesture. Don't just mechanically transform.

**This piece exists to test one idea:** Does statefulness make generated music feel composed?
